# The Velveteen Rabbit

**Author:** Margery Williams (sometimes credited as Margery Williams Bianco)
**Illustrator:** William Nicholson
**First published:** 1922
**Grade band:** Grade 2 / Ages 5–9
**Format:** Illustrated longer picture-book / short story

---

## Premise

A toy **velveteen rabbit** is given to a small boy on Christmas morning. At first he is loved and then forgotten, but a wise old toy called the **Skin Horse** explains to him what it means to become **Real**: when a child loves you for a very long time, not just to play with, but really loves you. Through years of being carried, hugged, and slept-on, the velveteen rabbit becomes the Boy's favourite. After the Boy falls ill with **scarlet fever** and the rabbit is ordered burned, a magical **Nursery Magic Fairy** appears and turns the worn-out toy into a real, living rabbit.

## Setting

A child's **nursery** in a big house, the **garden** of that house, and the **wood** at the bottom of the garden. The story moves through Christmas, summer in the garden, the Boy's illness, and the following spring. There is no specific town or country given; the setting is timeless and slightly old-fashioned.

## Characters

- **The Velveteen Rabbit** — the main character. A stuffed toy rabbit with a **brown-and-white spotted velveteen coat**, **thread whiskers**, and **pink-sateen-lined ears**. He starts the story as the "best of all" presents and ends it as a real wild rabbit.
- **The Boy** — never named. The child who is given the rabbit. He carries the rabbit everywhere, sleeps with him, and calls him **"Bunny."** He believes the rabbit is Real long before the magic happens.
- **The Skin Horse** — an old, wise, threadbare toy horse with most of his **tail hairs missing** and his **brown coat rubbed bald** in places. He is the oldest toy in the nursery and explains the magic of being Real.
- **Nana** — the nursery caretaker / nanny. She tidies up the toys and gives the rabbit to the Boy at bedtime when his china dog is missing.
- **The Boy's doctor** — orders all of the Boy's toys to be burned after the scarlet fever.
- **Two wild rabbits** — appear in the garden during one summer. They are alive, furry, and able to hop. They mock the velveteen rabbit for not having hind legs that work.
- **The Nursery Magic Fairy** — beautiful, appears from a flower at the end. She turns old, well-loved toys into Real animals.

## Plot in order

1. **Christmas morning.** The Velveteen Rabbit is found in the Boy's **Christmas stocking**, with a **sprig of holly** between his paws. He is fat and bunchy, with a spotted velveteen coat, real thread whiskers, and pink-lined ears.
2. **The first day.** The Boy loves him "for at least two hours," then is distracted by louder, more mechanical Christmas presents (a clockwork mouse, a model boat, and others). The rabbit is forgotten and put away in the nursery cupboard.
3. **Life with the other toys.** The mechanical toys in the nursery look down on the rabbit because he is only made of velveteen. He is very shy.
4. **Meeting the Skin Horse.** The oldest toy in the nursery is the **Skin Horse**, who has lost most of his tail and whose coat is rubbed bare. The rabbit asks him: **What is REAL?**
5. **The lesson.** The Skin Horse explains: **"Real isn't how you are made. It's a thing that happens to you. When a child loves you for a long, long time, not just to play with, but REALLY loves you, then you become Real."** He warns that it takes a long time, that it usually means your hair has been loved off and your eyes drop out — but once you are Real, you cannot be ugly to the person who loves you.
6. **Nana hands him over.** One bedtime, the Boy cannot find his china dog. **Nana** snatches the velveteen rabbit out of the cupboard and gives him to the Boy.
7. **In the Boy's bed.** From that night the rabbit sleeps in the Boy's bed every night. The Boy hugs him tight, makes tunnels under the blankets for him, and takes him everywhere.
8. **Becoming worn and beautiful.** Through years of being loved, the rabbit becomes **shabby**. His **velveteen fur grows shabby**, his **tail unsews**, his **pink nose is rubbed off** with kisses, and his **whiskers** are loved away. But to the Boy he is more beautiful than ever.
9. **"He's REAL!"** One day, the Boy proudly tells Nana about his rabbit: **"He isn't a toy. He's REAL!"** From this moment the rabbit knows that the Skin Horse was right.
10. **In the garden.** One summer day, the Boy leaves the rabbit on the grass and goes off to play. Two **real wild rabbits** appear in the grass. They have furry hind legs and can **hop**.
11. **Asked to dance.** The wild rabbits ask the velveteen rabbit to come and dance with them. He cannot move; he has no working hind legs. He says he doesn't want to. They laugh at him for being not a real rabbit and hop off.
12. **The Boy gets ill.** Later, the Boy catches **scarlet fever**. He is very sick for a long time and lies in bed. The rabbit stays with him through the fever, hidden in the bedclothes.
13. **The doctor's order.** When the Boy is on the way to getting better, the **doctor** insists that all the things in his bed — including the rabbit — must be **burned**, because they have been near the fever.
14. **The rubbish heap.** The velveteen rabbit is shut into a **sack** with old toys and picture books and dropped behind the **fowl-house** in the garden, waiting for a bonfire in the morning.
15. **A real tear.** In the night the rabbit wriggles out of the sack and lies on the grass, sad and lonely. **A real tear** falls from his eye into the earth.
16. **A magic flower.** Where the tear falls, a flower grows with a **golden cup-like blossom**. Out of the flower steps the **Nursery Magic Fairy**.
17. **The Fairy speaks.** She says she is the **Nursery Magic Fairy**, and that she comes for **toys that have been loved so much they are old and worn out**. She tells the rabbit: **"You were Real to the Boy because he loved you. Now you shall be Real to everyone."**
18. **The transformation.** The Fairy **kisses** the velveteen rabbit. He suddenly has **real fur, real hind legs, and a real nose**. He is a living rabbit.
19. **The moonlit wood.** She carries him into the wood at the bottom of the garden and sets him down with the other wild rabbits.
20. **Spring again.** The following spring, when the Boy is well again and playing in the garden, **two rabbits** come out of the bracken. One has odd markings on his back, **as if long ago he had been spotted**. The Boy looks at him — but never knows that this is his old velveteen Bunny, **come back to see the child who had first helped him to be Real**.

## Key objects and props

- **The Christmas stocking** with the rabbit and the sprig of holly inside.
- **The toy cupboard** in the nursery.
- **The blankets** the Boy makes into rabbit-burrow tunnels.
- **The garden** where the rabbit meets the wild rabbits.
- **The bed** where the Boy spends scarlet fever.
- **The sack of old toys** behind the fowl-house.
- **The golden flower** that grows from the rabbit's tear.
- **The Nursery Magic Fairy's kiss** — the moment of becoming real.

## Themes and ideas

- **Real love makes you Real.** The Skin Horse's speech is the heart of the book.
- **Being loved costs you.** Hair worn off, ears chewed, nose kissed away — the Boy's love is shown by how worn the rabbit becomes.
- **The pain of being left behind.** The rabbit is left on the rubbish heap to be burned; only then does the magic come.
- **Other rabbits.** A whole second world of wild rabbits exists outside the nursery; in the end the velveteen rabbit joins them.
- **Memory.** The Boy never learns what happened to his old rabbit, but the rabbit comes back to look at him.

## Tone and style

A slightly old-fashioned, gentle, slow story with a quietly sad middle and a magical ending. The narrator speaks directly to the reader. The vocabulary is more advanced than most early-grade picture books (**velveteen, sateen, threadbare, fowl-house, nursery, bracken**). William Nicholson's illustrations are warm and a little misty. The book is often read aloud as a chapter-book-length picture book.
