# The Cat in the Hat

**Author and Illustrator:** Dr. Seuss (Theodor Seuss Geisel)
**First published:** 1957
**Grade band:** Grade K / Ages 4–7
**Format:** Rhyming picture book / beginning reader (Beginner Books series)

---

## Premise

On a cold, wet, boring day, a brother and his little sister **Sally** are stuck at home with **nothing to do**. Their **mother is out**. Suddenly an enormous **Cat in a tall red-and-white striped hat** lets himself in and promises to entertain them. He balances a teetering tower of objects in the living room, brings out a big red box with two wild creatures called **Thing One** and **Thing Two** inside it, and turns the whole house upside down. The children's pet **fish** spends the whole book trying to make the Cat stop. Just before Mother gets home, the Cat returns in a strange cleaning machine and tidies everything up — leaving the children to decide whether to tell their mother what happened.

## Setting

A child's home on a **cold, wet** rainy day. The story takes place almost entirely **inside one house** — the front room, the hallway, the stairs. Outside, rain keeps falling.

## Characters

- **The narrator** — a young boy. Not named in the book. Sally's older brother.
- **Sally** — his little sister. Also home alone.
- **The fish** — the children's pet, in a **glass bowl**. The voice of caution. He keeps telling the Cat to leave.
- **The Cat in the Hat** — a very tall cat wearing a **red and white striped top hat**, a **red bow tie**, and **white gloves**. Mischievous, polite, full of tricks.
- **Thing One and Thing Two** — small blue-haired creatures with red suits and the numbers **"1"** and **"2"** on their chests. Wild and uncontrollable.
- **Mother** — never appears on the page. She is out for the day and comes home at the end.

## Plot in order

1. **A boring rainy day.** The narrator and his little sister **Sally** sit in their living room. It is **too wet to go out and too cold to play ball**. They are bored, sad, and home alone.
2. **A bump.** They hear a sudden loud **BUMP**, and in walks an enormous cat — **The Cat in the Hat**.
3. **An offer.** The Cat tells them he knows lots of good games and good tricks. He will show them. The children's **fish** in his bowl insists the Cat should **leave at once**.
4. **Balancing tricks.** The Cat ignores the fish. He picks up the fish in his bowl and **balances him on the tip of an umbrella**. He balances a cake, books, milk, a fan, a toy ship, more and more — all while standing on a **ball**. Then he falls over and everything comes crashing down.
5. **The Big Red Box.** The Cat promises more fun. He brings in a great big **red wooden box**.
6. **Thing One and Thing Two.** Out of the box come two small wild creatures with **blue hair** in **red suits**, with **"Thing 1"** and **"Thing 2"** on their chests.
7. **Wild chaos.** Thing One and Thing Two **fly kites inside the house**, knock over furniture, tangle their kite strings on the curtains and pictures, and ruin the new dress of the children's **mother**.
8. **The fish panics.** The pet fish suddenly sees out of the window that **mother is coming up the path** to the house. He shouts the warning.
9. **A net.** The narrator grabs a **net** and catches both Thing One and Thing Two. The Cat is embarrassed and puts them back in the **big red box**.
10. **The cleaning machine.** The house is a complete wreck. Just as the children are despairing, the Cat returns with a strange machine of many arms and brooms.
11. **Tidied up.** The Cat's cleaning machine sweeps and tidies everything back into place. The house looks perfect again. The Cat tips his hat to the children and leaves.
12. **Mother is home.** Mother comes through the door. She asks the children what they have been doing all day.
13. **A question for the reader.** The narrator is not sure how to answer. The book ends by asking the reader directly: **"What would YOU do if your mother asked you?"**

## Key objects and props

- **The Cat's red and white striped top hat** — the most famous hat in children's literature.
- **The Cat's red bow tie and white gloves.**
- **The umbrella** the Cat uses to balance things.
- **The fish in its bowl** — moved all over the house by the Cat.
- **The Cat's ball** that he stands on while juggling.
- **The big red wooden box** containing Thing One and Thing Two.
- **The two kites** flown inside the house.
- **Mother's new gown** that gets ruined.
- **The narrator's net** that catches the Things.
- **The Cat's cleaning machine** with many arms.

## Themes and ideas

- **Boredom and mischief.** The Cat arrives because the children have nothing to do.
- **Rules and breaking rules.** Mother is out; the Cat does everything she would forbid.
- **Cleaning up your mess.** The Cat insists, before he leaves, that he will fix the chaos he caused.
- **Honesty.** The book ends with the open question of whether to tell mother the truth.
- **A pet who tries to be sensible.** The fish is the voice of caution all the way through.

## Tone and style

Bouncy rhyming verse on every page. Bright, simple, cartoonish illustrations in red, white, blue, and black. The narrator speaks in short sentences. The book famously uses a very small vocabulary — Dr. Seuss wrote it to prove a children's beginning reader could be told with around 200 words.
