# Henry and Mudge: The First Book

**Author:** Cynthia Rylant
**Illustrator:** Suçie Stevenson
**First published:** 1987
**Grade band:** Grade 1 / Ages 5–7
**Format:** Early chapter book (seven short chapters)
**Chapter titles, in order:** *Henry, Mudge, Henry, Mudge, Mudge, Henry, Henry and Mudge*

---

## Premise

The first book in the long Henry and Mudge series. A lonely little boy named **Henry** has no brothers or sisters and no other children on his street. His parents agree he can have a dog. Henry picks out a puppy he calls **Mudge**, and Mudge grows into an enormous **180-pound** dog. The book follows them from picking the puppy out, through Mudge growing up, through the day Mudge gets lost and Henry has to look for him.

## Setting

Henry's neighbourhood — a street where Henry is the only child. The action moves between Henry's house and yard, the walk Henry and Mudge take together each day, and the wider neighbourhood where Mudge wanders off in the "lost" chapter. The setting is small, suburban, and gentle.

## Characters

- **Henry** — a small boy. Only child. No other children on his street, which is why he is lonely.
- **Mudge** — Henry's dog. Begins as a puppy and grows huge: **180 pounds**, **about three feet tall**, with **straight fur** and **floppy ears**. He drools.
- **Henry's parents** — mentioned. They eventually say yes to a dog.

No other named human characters appear.

## Plot in order

1. **Henry is lonely.** Henry has no brothers, no sisters, and no other children live on his street. He has nobody to play with.
2. **Henry asks for a dog.** Henry asks his parents for a dog. After a while, they say yes.
3. **Choosing a dog.** Henry has strong opinions about what kind of dog he wants. He does **not** want a dog with **curly hair**. He does **not** want a dog with **pointy ears**. He does not want a small dog.
4. **Meeting Mudge.** Henry finds a dog that is the opposite of those things — a dog with **straight fur** and **floppy ears**. He names him **Mudge**.
5. **Mudge grows.** As a puppy Mudge is small, but he grows very fast. He keeps growing out of his collars — **seven collars** in total before he reaches his full size.
6. **Mudge full size.** When Mudge is grown he weighs **180 pounds** and stands about **three feet tall**. He drools. He has paws like teacups (the book describes him as huge in every way).
7. **Walking to school together.** Once Henry has Mudge, walking down the street is no longer scary or lonely — Henry has his big dog beside him. Henry stops worrying about things.
8. **A daily worry.** Every morning, Henry walks to school. He worries while he is at school that Mudge will not be there when he gets home. Every day Mudge is there.
9. **Mudge gets lost.** One day Mudge goes off on his own and does not come back. Henry searches everywhere for him.
10. **Both are sad.** While Henry is looking, Mudge is also lost and sad, missing Henry just as much as Henry misses him.
11. **They find each other.** Eventually Henry and Mudge are reunited.
12. **A promise.** Henry hugs Mudge and promises he will never go off without him again. Mudge thinks the same about Henry. They are happy together and the book ends with them as best friends.

## Key objects and props

- **Seven collars** — the collars Mudge grows out of as he grows up.
- **Mudge's drool** — a recurring detail.
- **Mudge's floppy ears and straight fur** — what Henry was looking for.
- **The walk to school** — repeated almost every day, the daily ritual of Henry and Mudge.

## Themes and ideas

- **Loneliness and friendship.** Henry is the only child on his street; Mudge is the friend that fills that gap.
- **A boy and his dog.** The book is a quiet love story about a child and the pet that changes his world.
- **Worry and reassurance.** Henry worries while he's away from Mudge; the book reassures him (and the reader) that Mudge is always there.
- **Loss and reunion.** The "Mudge is missing" chapter is the emotional centre. Both Henry and Mudge feel the same way — apart they are sad, together they are happy.
- **Growing up.** Mudge grows enormously fast. The seven collars are a marker of just how much he changes.

## Tone and style

Very short chapters made of short, simple sentences — designed for a new reader to manage on their own. Soft, warm watercolour illustrations of a small boy and a giant, friendly, droopy-eared dog. The book is quiet and reassuring rather than exciting; even the "lost dog" chapter is gentle.
