# Because of Winn-Dixie

**Author:** Kate DiCamillo
**First published:** 2000
**Grade band:** Grade 3 / Ages 8–11
**Format:** Children's novel

---

## Premise

Ten-year-old **India Opal Buloni** has just moved with her preacher father to a small town in Florida and feels lonely. One day at the **Winn-Dixie** supermarket, a big, scruffy stray dog tears around the produce aisle. To stop the manager from sending the dog to the pound, Opal claims him as hers and names him **Winn-Dixie**, after the store. Through Winn-Dixie, Opal slowly makes new friends — an old, nearly-blind woman named **Gloria Dump**, a kind librarian called **Miss Franny Block**, a quiet ex-convict pet-shop worker called **Otis**, a five-year-old named **Sweetie Pie**, a sad girl named **Amanda**, and two teasing brothers called the **Dewberry boys**. Along the way she gets her father to tell her **ten things** about the mother who left when Opal was three. The book ends with a community party at Gloria Dump's house, a thunderstorm, and Opal finally beginning to understand and accept where her family is.

## Setting

**Naomi, Florida** — a small, hot Southern town. Opal and her father live in the **Friendly Corners Trailer Park**. The story moves between their trailer, the **Open Arms Baptist Church** where her father preaches, the local **Winn-Dixie** supermarket, the small **library**, the **Gertrude's Pets** pet shop, and **Gloria Dump's overgrown garden** with her famous "**bottle tree**."

## Characters

- **India Opal Buloni** — the ten-year-old narrator. Curious, watchful, lonely, and a bit angry about her mother leaving.
- **The Preacher** — Opal's father, the new preacher at the **Open Arms Baptist Church**. Opal sometimes calls him "the preacher" because he is so focused on his work. Quiet, gentle, sad.
- **Opal's mother** — never appears. She **left when Opal was three**. The book is partly about Opal trying to learn who she was.
- **Winn-Dixie** — a large, scruffy, smiley dog with a "pathological fear of thunderstorms." He is everything that brings the new friends together.
- **Miss Franny Block** — the elderly **town librarian**. She tells Opal a long story about how, as a young woman, she once fought off a **bear** in the library with a copy of **War and Peace**.
- **Gloria Dump** — a tall, nearly **blind** old woman. She lives in a small house with an overgrown yard and a **bottle tree** — a tree she has hung with empty whiskey and beer bottles to "catch" the ghosts of all her past mistakes. She is a recovering alcoholic. She becomes Opal's deepest friend.
- **Otis** — a shy young man with a guitar who runs **Gertrude's Pets**, the local pet shop. He spent time in prison (a small misunderstanding). At night he lets the animals out of their cages and plays guitar to them.
- **Sweetie Pie Thomas** — a five-year-old neighbour who keeps **sucking her knuckle** and wants a dog of her own.
- **Amanda Wilkinson** — a sad girl Opal's age. Her **little brother Carson** drowned the year before.
- **Stevie and Dunlap Dewberry** — two brothers who tease Opal and call her "preacher's daughter." She eventually softens towards them.

## Plot in order

1. **A new town.** Opal and her father have just moved to **Naomi, Florida**, where he is the new preacher at the **Open Arms Baptist Church**. They live in the **Friendly Corners Trailer Park**.
2. **An errand to the Winn-Dixie.** Opal's father sends her to the **Winn-Dixie** for a box of macaroni and cheese, two tomatoes, and white rice.
3. **A dog in the produce.** Inside the store, Opal sees a **big, ugly, friendly dog** tearing up the produce. The manager wants to send him to the pound. Opal shouts that the dog is hers — and on the spot names him **Winn-Dixie**.
4. **Taking him home.** Opal walks Winn-Dixie home and tells her father about him. The Preacher says she can keep him.
5. **The ten things.** Opal asks her father to tell her **ten things about her mother** — one for every year of her life. He does. They include:
   - she had **red hair and freckles**,
   - she could **make anyone laugh**,
   - she could **grow anything**,
   - she was a **fast runner**,
   - she was a **terrible cook**,
   - she **loved stories**,
   - she knew **all the constellations**,
   - she hated being a **preacher's wife**,
   - she **drank**,
   - and she **loved Opal very, very much**.
   Opal memorises the list.
6. **The library and the bear.** Opal walks into the small town library with Winn-Dixie. Winn-Dixie scares the elderly librarian, **Miss Franny Block**. Miss Franny tells Opal the long story of how, as a girl, she once **chased a bear out of the library with a copy of War and Peace**.
7. **Gloria Dump.** The Dewberry boys tell Opal that an old woman called **Gloria Dump** is a witch. Opal goes to see for herself. Gloria is nearly **blind** but kind, with a huge **bottle tree** in her yard. They begin a daily friendship — Opal **reads aloud** to her from books like *Gone with the Wind*.
8. **Sweetie Pie.** Opal meets a five-year-old called **Sweetie Pie Thomas** who keeps sucking her knuckle.
9. **Otis and the pet shop.** Opal gets a job at **Gertrude's Pets** sweeping up. She meets **Otis**, who is quiet and a little odd. One morning she finds him sitting on the floor with his **guitar**, surrounded by **escaped rabbits, birds, and other animals** that he has let out of their cages. She learns later that Otis was in **prison** for hitting a policeman.
10. **The Littmus Lozenge.** Miss Franny tells the story of her **great-grandfather**, who fought in the Civil War and afterwards invented the **Littmus Lozenge** — a candy that tastes both sweet and sad, "like the sorrow of the world." Opal shares the candy with everyone in her life, and each friend tastes the sadness in it.
11. **Amanda Wilkinson.** A girl Opal's age, **Amanda**, has been silent and sad for a long time. Opal slowly learns that Amanda's little brother, **Carson**, **drowned** the year before.
12. **The party.** Opal and Gloria plan a **community party** in Gloria's garden — sandwiches, lemonade, music, and all of Opal's new friends invited: Miss Franny, Otis, Sweetie Pie, Amanda, the Dewberry brothers, and her father.
13. **The thunderstorm.** Just as the party gets going, a **thunderstorm** rolls in. Everyone runs inside. Opal suddenly realises that **Winn-Dixie**, who is **terrified of thunder**, is **missing**.
14. **The search.** Opal and her father drive around in the rain looking for Winn-Dixie. As they search, Opal finally tells her father how much she misses her mother. He tells her that her mother is not coming back, that she needs Opal to know that, and that he is not going to stop loving her.
15. **Found.** They return to Gloria Dump's house. Inside, hidden in Gloria's bedroom from the thunder, is **Winn-Dixie**. Opal realises that her dog had been there the whole time.
16. **Together.** The book ends with the party continuing inside, with all of Opal's new friends together, singing along to Otis's guitar. Opal recites her **ten things about her mother** to Winn-Dixie one last time — and the next day, she begins to look at her new life in Naomi with hope.

## Key objects and props

- **The Winn-Dixie store** and the **dog Winn-Dixie**.
- **The Preacher's list of ten things** about Opal's mother.
- **Miss Franny's copy of War and Peace** — used to chase a bear.
- **Gloria Dump's bottle tree** — empty bottles tied to a tree to "catch the ghosts" of her past.
- **Otis's guitar** in the pet shop.
- **The Littmus Lozenge** — the candy that tastes "sorrowful but sweet."
- **The party in Gloria's garden** — sandwiches, lemonade, music, and a sudden storm.
- **The thunderstorm** — Winn-Dixie's biggest fear.

## Themes and ideas

- **Lonely people finding each other.** Almost every character in this book is missing somebody. They form a small community by accident.
- **Living with loss.** Opal's mother is gone. Amanda's brother is gone. Miss Franny's youth is gone. Gloria's sober past is gone. The book is honest about sadness.
- **Letting go.** Gloria's bottle tree, the Littmus Lozenge that tastes of sadness, and the preacher's words to Opal in the rain all teach the same lesson.
- **Not judging people.** Otis is an ex-convict but is kind. Gloria looks like a witch but is wise.
- **A dog as a bridge.** Almost every friendship in the book happens **because of Winn-Dixie**.

## Tone and style

A gentle, slow, beautifully written Southern children's novel. Short chapters. Opal narrates in first person in a clear, plain voice. The book moves at the pace of one Florida summer. The mood is melancholy in places but the tone is always warm.
