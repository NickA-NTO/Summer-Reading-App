# The Gingerbread Man

**Author:** Traditional folktale, retold for Usborne
**Original printed version:** Published in *St. Nicholas Magazine*, May 1875
**Grade band:** Grade K / Ages 4–6
**Format:** Illustrated retelling for early readers (Usborne)

---

## Premise

An **old woman** bakes a **gingerbread man** to give herself and her **old husband** something sweet to eat. The moment she opens the oven door, the gingerbread man **jumps out** and runs away. He runs through the village and into the fields, calling back over his shoulder a famous taunting rhyme — **"Run, run, as fast as you can! You can't catch me, I'm the Gingerbread Man!"** He outruns the old woman, the old man, and a series of farm animals. Then he comes to a wide **river** he cannot cross. A clever **fox** offers to carry him across — and tricks him into climbing higher and higher up his body until, with a snap of the jaws, the fox **eats him up**.

## Setting

A country village and the fields around it. The story moves from the **kitchen** (where the gingerbread man is baked), out through the **garden gate**, along the road, past **farm animals**, and finally to the bank of a **river** that the gingerbread man cannot cross on his own.

## Characters

- **The Gingerbread Man** — a freshly baked gingerbread biscuit shaped like a small running man. He has **currants for eyes**, a **cherry for a mouth**, and **icing buttons**.
- **The little old woman** — the baker. Wants the gingerbread man for tea.
- **The little old man** — her husband. Joins the chase.
- **Farm animals** — a **cow**, a **horse**, and other animals along the road (the exact list varies in retellings).
- **The fox** — a cunning red fox who waits by the river and ferries the gingerbread man across.

## Plot in order

1. **A gingerbread man baked.** A little old woman and a little old man have no children. She decides to bake a man-shaped gingerbread biscuit to keep them company at tea. She gives him **currant eyes, a cherry mouth, and icing buttons** down his front.
2. **He jumps from the oven.** The moment she opens the oven door, the gingerbread man **leaps out** and runs out of the kitchen and out of the house.
3. **The chase begins.** The little old woman and the little old man run after him, shouting "Stop! Stop!"
4. **The taunt.** The gingerbread man laughs and calls out behind him:
   **"Run, run, as fast as you can! You can't catch me, I'm the Gingerbread Man!"**
5. **Past the animals.** As he runs through the fields, he passes a **cow** and a **horse** (and in some retellings other farm animals). Each one tries to catch him, but each time he repeats his rhyme and outruns them.
6. **The river.** Eventually the gingerbread man reaches a wide **river**. He cannot swim. The crowd of chasers is catching up behind him.
7. **The fox.** A clever **fox** comes out of the bushes and offers to **carry the gingerbread man across the river**. The fox seems friendly. The gingerbread man agrees.
8. **Climbing higher.** The gingerbread man jumps on the fox's **tail**. As they get further into the river the fox says he is **getting wet** and that the gingerbread man should climb up onto his **back**.
9. **Higher still.** A little further across, the fox says the gingerbread man should climb up onto his **shoulders**.
10. **Onto the nose.** As they near the far bank, the fox says the water is splashing too high — the gingerbread man should jump onto his **nose**.
11. **The snap.** As soon as the gingerbread man is balanced on the fox's nose, the fox **tosses him into the air**, **opens his jaws**, and **eats him in one bite**.
12. **The end.** "I am a quarter gone… I am half gone… I am three-quarters gone… I am all gone!" The gingerbread man is eaten up. The chase is over.

## Key objects and props

- **The hot oven** the gingerbread man jumps out of.
- **The gingerbread man's currant eyes, cherry mouth, and icing buttons.**
- **The garden gate** he runs through.
- **The road / fields** he runs along.
- **The river** he cannot cross.
- **The fox's tail, back, shoulders, and nose** — each progressively higher.

## Themes and ideas

- **Pride goes before a fall.** The gingerbread man brags about how fast he is — until one slow, clever animal outsmarts him.
- **Cleverness beats speed.** The fox is much slower than the gingerbread man, but his trick wins.
- **Don't trust strangers.** The fox offers help but has plans of his own.
- **A cumulative story.** The same rhyme repeats every time a new chaser appears.
- **A traditional shape for a fairy tale.** A magical creature is made, comes to life, runs away, gets eaten — a quick, neat folktale.

## Tone and style

A short, fast, repeating retelling. The famous rhyme appears again and again — the perfect line for children to chant along to. Bright cartoony illustrations show a smiling gingerbread biscuit running through the countryside with a long line of pursuers behind him.

## Quick fact bank (useful for question writing)

- The little old woman bakes a gingerbread man because: she and the old man want something to eat.
- What the gingerbread man has for **eyes**: **currants** (raisins).
- What he has for a **mouth**: a **cherry**.
- What he has down his front: **icing buttons**.
- What the gingerbread man does the moment the oven is opened: he **jumps out and runs away**.
- The rhyme the gingerbread man chants as he runs: **"Run, run, as fast as you can! You can't catch me, I'm the Gingerbread Man!"**
- The first two chasers: the **little old woman** and the **little old man**.
- Farm animals he runs past on the way: a **cow**, a **horse** (and other farm animals in different retellings).
- What stops the gingerbread man from running any further: a wide **river** he cannot cross.
- The animal who offers to carry him across the river: a **fox**.
- The order the gingerbread man climbs up the fox: **tail → back → shoulders → nose**.
- What the fox does when the gingerbread man is on his nose: he **tosses him up and eats him**.
- The moral of the story: **pride and trusting strangers can be dangerous** — clever animals can outsmart fast ones.
- The story's origin: a **traditional folktale**, first printed in *St. Nicholas Magazine* in 1875.
