# There Is a Bird on Your Head!

**Author and Illustrator:** Mo Willems
**First published:** 2007
**Grade band:** Pre-K / Beginning reader
**Format:** Easy-reader picture book (part of the Elephant & Piggie series; Theodor Seuss Geisel Award winner)

---

## Premise

**Gerald** the elephant goes for a walk and a **bird** lands on his head. Piggie tells him this, calmly. Soon there is **another bird**. The two birds turn out to be a couple — they **fall in love** and build a **nest** on Gerald's head. They lay **three eggs**. The eggs hatch. Now there is a whole **bird family** on Gerald's head, and he is desperate. Piggie has a very simple idea: just **ask the birds to please leave**. Gerald, after a lot of hesitation, does — and the birds happily go. The book's last joke: they all move from Gerald's head to **Piggie's** head.

## Setting

Outdoors, on a plain white background. The whole book happens in the space between Gerald and Piggie. The only setting that really matters is Gerald's head (and at the end, Piggie's head).

## Characters

- **Gerald** — the elephant. Has the bird problem. Worries about everything.
- **Piggie** — the pig. Watches the whole thing, mostly amused. Has the obvious solution that Gerald never thinks of.
- **Two birds** — a male and a female. They are calm, friendly, and very domestic.
- **Three baby birds** — hatch from the eggs near the end of the book.

## Plot in order

1. **"There is a bird on your head."** Piggie tells Gerald, very plainly. Gerald is horrified.
2. **A second bird.** A second bird lands on top of the first one. Now there are **two birds** on Gerald's head.
3. **They fall in love.** The two birds clearly **like each other** very much. They are a couple.
4. **A nest.** The birds build a **nest** on Gerald's head.
5. **Three eggs.** The female bird lays **three eggs** in the nest.
6. **The eggs hatch.** Soon the eggs hatch, and three **baby birds** are born — right on top of Gerald.
7. **A bird family.** Gerald now has a full **family of birds** living on his head. He is at the end of his rope.
8. **Piggie's simple idea.** Piggie suggests that Gerald just **ask the birds to leave**.
9. **Gerald asks politely.** Gerald turns to the birds and **asks them, very politely, if they would please go somewhere else**.
10. **The birds say yes.** The bird family is happy to oblige. They lift off Gerald's head…
11. **…and onto Piggie's head.** All of them — the two parent birds and the three baby birds — land **on top of Piggie**. The book ends with Piggie in the same trouble Gerald was just in.

## Key objects and props

- **The first bird** that lands on Gerald.
- **The second bird** that joins the first.
- **The nest** built on Gerald's head.
- **The three eggs** in the nest.
- **The three baby birds** that hatch from the eggs.

## Themes and ideas

- **Asking for what you want.** The whole book is one long joke about how Gerald could solve his problem by just **using his words**.
- **Patience and friendship.** Piggie watches all of this with quiet amusement. She does not panic.
- **Problems can be polite.** The birds are perfectly friendly. They only need to be asked.
- **Bad luck spreads.** The very last joke is that the problem just moves from one friend to the other.
- **Stay calm.** Piggie's calm voice is the contrast to Gerald's panic — and she is the one who solves it.

## Tone and style

Very short text in speech bubbles. White-page background. The whole comedy is in the **escalation**: bird → two birds → nest → eggs → babies → family. Gerald's face goes from confused to upset to terrified across the book. Piggie's face barely changes.

- Who has the bird problem: **Gerald**.
- The first thing Piggie tells Gerald: **"There is a bird on your head."**
- How many birds end up on Gerald's head before they start a family: **two**.
- What the two birds build on Gerald's head: a **nest**.
- How many eggs are laid in the nest: **three**.
- What comes out of the eggs: **three baby birds**.
- How many birds are on Gerald's head once the eggs hatch: a whole **family** (five birds in total).
- Piggie's simple suggestion: **ask the birds to leave** (politely).
- How Gerald gets the birds off his head: he **asks them nicely** to move.
- Where the bird family moves at the very end of the book: onto **Piggie's head**.
- The big idea: if something is bothering you, sometimes you just need to **use your words** and ask.
- The series this book is part of: **Elephant & Piggie**.
- The award this book won: a **Theodor Seuss Geisel Award** (medal for best beginning reader book).
