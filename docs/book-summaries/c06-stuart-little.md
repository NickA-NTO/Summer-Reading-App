# Stuart Little

**Author:** E. B. White
**Illustrator:** Garth Williams
**First published:** 1945
**Grade band:** Grade 3 / Ages 7–10
**Format:** Children's novel

---

## Premise

The Little family of **New York City** has a second baby — but he is only **two inches tall** and looks **exactly like a mouse**. They name him **Stuart**. The book follows Stuart from his unusual childhood at home into adventures across the city (rescuing a ring, racing a sailboat in Central Park, befriending a small bird called **Margalo**), and finally out of the city in a tiny car of his own, on a long open-ended journey to find his lost friend Margalo and see the country.

## Setting

The first half of the book is set in **New York City**: the Littles' apartment, **Central Park**, a sailboat pond, the city streets and rivers. The second half is on the open road, in small American towns, including one called **Ames Crossing** where Stuart briefly works as a teacher. Time is the present day (1940s).

## Characters

- **Stuart Little** — the main character. A small, polite, brave little boy born to a human family who looks like a mouse. He is **just over two inches tall** and wears a smart little suit and hat. Adventurous, well-mannered, and a good sailor.
- **Mr. Little** — Stuart's father.
- **Mrs. Little** — Stuart's mother. The owner of the **ring** Stuart fishes out of the drain.
- **George Little** — Stuart's older brother. A human-sized boy.
- **Snowbell** — the **family cat**. Sometimes friendly, sometimes a danger. Plots against Margalo.
- **Margalo** — a small **yellow bird (canary)** that the family finds **half-frozen on their windowsill**. She lives with the Littles and becomes Stuart's best friend.
- **The Angora cat** — a stranger cat that Snowbell teams up with in a plot to harm Margalo.
- **Dr. Carey** — a kindly **dentist** who owns the model sailboat **Wasp** and who later lends Stuart a **tiny model car**.
- **Harriet Ames** — a **fifteen-year-old girl** Stuart meets later in the story. She is the same size as Stuart but looks like a normal human girl. Stuart hopes she might be his sweetheart.

## Plot in order

1. **An unusual baby.** When the second Little child is born, he is only **two inches tall** and looks like a mouse. The family names him **Stuart**.
2. **Growing up small.** Stuart lives in the same apartment as his parents, his brother **George**, and the family cat **Snowbell**. His parents make him tiny clothes and a tiny bed.
3. **The ring in the drain.** Mrs. Little drops her **wedding ring** down the **bathroom sink drain**. Stuart volunteers and is **lowered down on a string** into the drain pipe to fetch it.
4. **Inside the piano.** When the family's piano keys stick, Stuart **climbs inside the piano** to push the hammers back into place while George plays.
5. **The window shade.** One morning Stuart is **rolled up inside a window shade**. The family eventually finds him.
6. **The garbage scow.** Stuart is accidentally **dumped into a garbage truck** and carried off to a **garbage scow** floating out into the East River. He is rescued.
7. **Margalo arrives.** One winter the Littles find a small **yellow bird** on their windowsill, half-frozen, and bring her inside. Her name is **Margalo**. She becomes part of the family and Stuart's closest friend.
8. **The sailboat race.** Stuart goes to **Central Park** to watch a sailboat race on the pond. He meets the kindly **Dr. Carey**, who owns a model boat called the **Wasp** but whose usual sailor has not turned up. Dr. Carey takes Stuart on as captain. Stuart sails the Wasp against a rival boat called the **Lillian B. Womrath** and wins the race.
9. **The cats' plot.** Snowbell makes friends with an **Angora cat** from the street. Together they plot to kill and eat Margalo.
10. **Margalo flies away.** Margalo is warned about the plot and **flies away in the night** without saying goodbye.
11. **Stuart sets off.** Stuart is heartbroken. He decides he will **leave home** to find Margalo. Dr. Carey lends him a special little car: a **gasoline-powered model car** that turns invisible at the press of a button.
12. **On the road.** Stuart drives out of New York and along country roads, north into the open countryside.
13. **A day as a teacher.** In one small town, Stuart serves as a **substitute teacher** for a day. He talks to the class about being a good chairman of the world.
14. **Ames Crossing.** In a small town called **Ames Crossing**, Stuart meets **Harriet Ames**, a **fifteen-year-old girl** who is exactly his size but otherwise a normal human girl. They plan a canoe trip on the river.
15. **A disappointment.** Stuart waits for Harriet at the river — but the little canoe he had hidden has been damaged. He is bitterly disappointed and the picnic falls through.
16. **Back on the road.** Stuart drives on. A telephone repairman tells him he has seen a small bird like Margalo flying north.
17. **The open ending.** The book ends with Stuart still on his way **north**, looking for Margalo, **never giving up**. The reader never finds out if he finds her — it is one of the most famous open endings in children's literature.

## Key objects and props

- **Mrs. Little's wedding ring** — fished out of the sink drain.
- **The piano** Stuart climbs inside.
- **The window shade** he gets rolled up in.
- **The model sailboat Wasp** — the boat Stuart captains in Central Park.
- **The rival boat Lillian B. Womrath**.
- **Margalo's small nest** at the Littles' apartment.
- **Dr. Carey's tiny gasoline car** with an invisibility button.
- **Stuart's smart little suit and hat**.

## Themes and ideas

- **Being different.** Stuart is the right son of his family but the wrong size and shape. The book lets him be both ordinary and extraordinary.
- **Adventure.** From a sink drain to the open road, the book is one trip after another.
- **Friendship and loss.** Margalo's disappearance is the engine of the second half of the book.
- **Hope without a guaranteed ending.** Stuart never gives up looking. The book never tells us whether he finds her.
- **The dignity of small things.** Stuart is brave, polite, and serious — the book treats his tiny life with full weight.

## Tone and style

A short, calm, warm novel in clear straightforward sentences. The narrator is gentle and matter-of-fact about every miracle, which makes the book very funny. Garth Williams's pen-and-ink drawings of a mouse-sized boy in a suit and hat are part of the book's classic feel.
