# The Magic Faraway Tree

**Author:** Enid Blyton
**First published:** 1943 (second book in the Faraway Tree series)
**Grade band:** Grade 2 / Ages 7–10
**Format:** Children's chapter book

---

## Premise

Three children — **Joe, Beth, and Frannie** — live in a small country cottage at the edge of an **Enchanted Wood**. In the wood grows an enormous, magical tree called the **Faraway Tree**, whose branches reach up into the clouds. At the very top of the tree there is a **ladder leading up through a hole in the cloud**, and on the other side, a different **magical land** arrives every few days and then moves on. Strange, wonderful creatures live in the tree itself. In this second book of the series, the children's cousin **Rick** (called Dick in older editions) comes to stay, and together with their friends in the tree they have adventures in many of the magical lands at the top, including the **Land of Topsy-Turvy**, the **Land of Goodies**, and the **Land of Birthdays**.

## Setting

A country cottage and the **Enchanted Wood** around it. Inside the wood stands the giant **Faraway Tree**. The story moves between:

- **The children's cottage** at the edge of the wood.
- **The trunk of the Faraway Tree**, where the magical folk live in carved-out rooms.
- **The top of the tree**, where the ladder goes up through a cloud.
- **Different magical lands** at the top — a new one each time the children climb up.

## Characters

### The children
- **Joe** — the oldest child, sensible and brave.
- **Beth** — the middle child, kind and motherly.
- **Frannie** — the youngest girl, lively and curious.
- **Rick** (Dick in older editions) — the children's **cousin**, who comes to stay. Sometimes a little greedy or clumsy and gets the others into trouble.

### Magical folk who live in the tree
- **Moon-Face** — a happy creature with a **round, beaming face like a moon**. He lives near the top of the tree, in a tiny house with the famous **slippery-slip slide** that goes down through the inside of the tree trunk all the way to the bottom.
- **Silky** — a lovely **fairy** with long golden hair. She lives in the tree and makes Pop Biscuits and Google Buns for the children.
- **The Saucepan Man** — a small man covered all over with **saucepans and kettles** that clatter when he moves. He is **deaf** and constantly mishears what people say, which is funny.
- **Dame Washalot** — an old lady halfway down the tree who **does washing all day** and tips her dirty water down the trunk, splashing everyone climbing up.
- **Mr. Watzisname** — sleeps with his mouth wide open and his name is so often forgotten that people just call him "Mr. What's-His-Name."
- **The Angry Pixie** — a small, bad-tempered pixie who lives in the tree. He gets furious if anyone peeks through his window and is famous for **throwing water out of his window** at intruders.

## How the magic works

- At the top of the Faraway Tree is a **little ladder that goes up through a hole in a cloud**.
- On the other side of the cloud, a **different magical land** is always waiting.
- Lands **change every few days**: the old one moves on and a new one arrives.
- You must climb back down **before the land moves on**, or you'll be **stuck there** until it comes round to the top of the tree again — which might be a very long time.
- Some lands are wonderful (Goodies, Birthdays, Take-What-You-Want). Some are nasty (Tempers, Dame Slap's school).

## Lands visited in this book

The children, the cousin, and their tree friends visit many magical lands over the course of the book. These include:

- **The Land of Topsy-Turvy** — everything is upside down: houses on their roofs, people standing on their heads.
- **The Land of Spells** — full of wizards and witches; spells can go wrong here.
- **The Land of Dreams** — where strange, dreamy things happen.
- **The Land of Do-As-You-Please** — where you can do absolutely anything you want (ride a tiger, drive a train).
- **The Land of Toys** — full of living toys.
- **The Land of Goodies** — houses made of cake, fences made of biscuits, hedges of toffee.
- **The Land of Magic Medicines** — visited when one of their family is sick.
- **The Land of Tempers** — full of angry people who shout. Friends are kidnapped from here.
- **The Land of Presents** — where everyone is given lovely presents.
- **The Land of Birthdays** — every day is somebody's birthday.

## Plot in order

1. **The cousin arrives.** Rick comes to stay with Joe, Beth, and Frannie. They cannot wait to show him the secret of the wood.
2. **Into the Enchanted Wood.** The children take Rick into the Enchanted Wood. They listen to the trees whispering **"Wisha-wisha-wisha"** and find the great Faraway Tree.
3. **Up the tree.** They climb the trunk, dodging **Dame Washalot's** falling washing-water and **the Angry Pixie's** kettle of water. They visit the houses of **Silky, Mr. Watzisname, the Saucepan Man,** and finally **Moon-Face** near the top.
4. **The slippery-slip.** Moon-Face shows them his **slippery-slip slide**: you sit on a cushion and slide down the inside of the tree all the way to the ground. It is the children's favourite way of leaving the tree.
5. **First adventure: Topsy-Turvy.** The first land the children take Rick to is the **Land of Topsy-Turvy**. They walk on their hands, drink tea standing on their heads, and have to leave quickly.
6. **Trouble in the lands.** Over many chapters, the children, Rick, and the tree folk go up the ladder into one strange land after another. Sometimes Rick is greedy or careless — for instance, in the **Land of Goodies** he eats too much from the houses — and they have to get away before they are caught.
7. **Mother is ill.** At one point the children's mother becomes ill, and they go to the **Land of Magic Medicines** to find a cure for her.
8. **Friends kidnapped.** **Moon-Face, Silky, and the Saucepan Man** are captured and taken into the **Land of Tempers**. The children mount a rescue and bring their friends back to the tree.
9. **A birthday.** At one point the **Land of Birthdays** arrives at the top of the tree, and the children have a wonderful birthday party there.
10. **Stuck in a land.** In one adventure, the **land moves on while they are still in it**, and they have to wait, half-frightened and half-excited, for the land to come back round so they can climb home.
11. **Home again.** Each chapter is a separate adventure, ending with the children sliding back down the slippery-slip to the ground and going home for tea.
12. **A summer of magic.** By the end of the book, Rick has had a whole summer of adventures and is sad to go home.

## Key objects and props

- **The Faraway Tree** itself, with rooms carved into its trunk.
- **The ladder at the top** going up through the cloud.
- **The slippery-slip slide** inside the trunk, used to get down from Moon-Face's house.
- **Moon-Face's cushions** that you sit on to go down the slide.
- **Silky's Pop Biscuits and Google Buns** — magical food the children eat in the tree.
- **The Saucepan Man's saucepans and kettles**, which clatter as he moves.
- **Dame Washalot's bucket of dirty water** that comes pouring down the trunk.
- **The Angry Pixie's kettle of water**, thrown out of his window.

## Themes and ideas

- **Wonder and imagination.** Each land at the top of the tree is a different daydream made real.
- **Friendship across the strange.** The children are friends with a fairy, a half-deaf saucepan man, and a moon-faced creature.
- **Rules in a magical world.** You can do almost anything, but you must leave before the land moves on. Magic has its own logic.
- **Kindness and rescue.** The children rescue their friends when they are captured.
- **A long, lazy childhood summer.** The whole book has the feel of school holidays.

## Tone and style

Short chapters, each almost a separate story. Warm, bright, gently old-fashioned British prose. Lots of made-up names and silly noises (Wisha-wisha-wisha; Slippery-slip). Modern editions update the children's names (**Joe, Beth, Frannie, Rick**) from the older ones (Jo, Bessie, Fanny, Dick). The vocabulary is straightforward; the magic does the heavy lifting.
