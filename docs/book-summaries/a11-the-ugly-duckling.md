# The Ugly Duckling

**Author:** Hans Christian Andersen (original story, 1843); retold in the catalog edition by **Jerry Pinkney** (1999)
**Grade band:** Grade 1 / Ages 4–8
**Format:** Illustrated picture book retelling of a classic fairy tale

---

## Premise

A large, grey, ugly duckling is mocked and chased away by every animal he meets. He travels through summer, autumn, and a terrible winter alone, sure that he is too ugly to belong anywhere. When spring finally comes, he discovers his own reflection and learns that he was never a duckling at all — he is a beautiful **swan**.

## Setting

The countryside. The story moves through several places in order:

- **A duck's nest by a river**, under big **burdock leaves**, near a farmhouse — where the story begins.
- **The farmyard** with hens, ducks, turkeys, and a poultry-girl.
- **A wild marsh / moor** with wild ducks and geese.
- **A little tumbledown cottage** in the marsh, lived in by an old woman, a tom cat, and a hen.
- **A pond / lake** through autumn.
- **A frozen pond** in winter, where the duckling is rescued and taken to a peasant's house.
- **A beautiful garden** in spring, with apple trees in blossom and three white swans.

## Characters

- **The ugly duckling** — the main character. Hatches from an unusually large egg. Big, grey, and clumsy as a "duckling"; in reality, he is a baby swan.
- **The mother duck** — sits on the eggs; takes the new ducklings out into the world; eventually gives up on her ugly child.
- **The other ducks, hens, and turkey-cock** at the farmyard who peck, hiss at, and attack him.
- **The poultry-girl** who looks after the farmyard birds and **kicks** the ugly duckling.
- **The wild ducks and wild geese** on the moor.
- **The hunters** with **guns** and **dogs** who come to the moor.
- **The old woman**, her **tom cat**, and her **hen** in the marsh cottage.
- **The peasant / farmer** who rescues the duckling from the ice.
- **The peasant's wife and children** at his house.
- **Three beautiful swans** the duckling sees in the spring garden.
- **The children** in the garden at the end, who admire the new swan.

## Plot in order

1. **Summer by the river.** A mother duck sits on her eggs in a quiet spot under the **burdock leaves** beside a river, near a farmhouse.
2. **The eggs hatch.** One by one the little ducklings crack out of their shells — but one **very large egg** is still left.
3. **The big egg hatches.** When the last egg finally cracks open, the duckling inside is **very large and very ugly**, grey and clumsy.
4. **The swim test.** The next day the mother takes her children to the water. They all swim — even the ugly one — so she decides he must be one of hers.
5. **The farmyard.** She takes them to the farmyard and teaches them to **bend their necks** and say "quack" to the old duck. She warns them to bow nicely.
6. **Pecked and chased.** The other ducks peck him, the **turkey-cock** puffs himself up and attacks him, the **hens** beat him, and the **poultry-girl** even **kicks him** with her foot.
7. **He runs away.** Sure that everyone is afraid of him "because I am ugly," the duckling escapes over the farmyard fence and flies into the world.
8. **The wild moor.** He arrives at a great **marsh / moor** where wild ducks live. They tolerate him but say he is very ugly.
9. **The hunters.** Suddenly there are gunshots. **Hunters with dogs** come hunting on the marsh. **Wild geese fall dead** around him. Big dogs splash by him. The duckling hides among the reeds and escapes by luck.
10. **The tumbledown cottage.** Lost in a storm, the duckling shelters in a small **cottage** in the marsh. An **old woman** lives there with her pet **tom cat** and her **hen**.
11. **Cat and hen.** The cat asks the duckling: **"Can you arch your back? Can you purr?"** The hen asks: **"Can you lay eggs?"** The duckling can do none of these things, so they tell him he is useless and he leaves.
12. **Autumn on the lake.** The duckling swims alone on a **pond** as autumn comes. He sees a flock of beautiful great white birds (swans) fly away south for the winter and longs to be like them, though he does not yet know what they are.
13. **The frozen pond.** Winter is bitter. The duckling swims in smaller and smaller circles as the ice closes around him until at last he is **frozen fast in the ice**.
14. **Rescue.** A **peasant** finds him, breaks the ice with his clog, and carries him home to his wife.
15. **Trouble at the peasant's house.** The peasant's **children** want to play with the duckling. He is so frightened that he flies up and crashes around the kitchen — splashing into the **milk pan**, then the **butter tub**, then the **flour barrel**, and finally out of the door.
16. **Surviving the winter.** The duckling spends the rest of the winter alone, half-frozen and miserable, in the reeds at the edge of a pond.
17. **Spring arrives.** One bright morning he tests his wings and finds he can fly. He flies into a **beautiful garden** with apple trees in blossom and a lake.
18. **The three swans.** On the water swim **three beautiful white swans**. The duckling is sure they are so beautiful that they will kill him for being so ugly. He bows his head down to the water, ready to die.
19. **The reflection.** In the mirror of the water he sees not a grey ugly duckling but **a graceful and beautiful swan** — himself.
20. **Welcomed at last.** The other swans swim around him and stroke him with their beaks. **Children come into the garden** and cry, **"The new one is the most beautiful of all!"** The old swans bow their heads to him.
21. **The final feeling.** The new swan ruffles his feathers, lifts his slender neck, and says to himself: **"I never dreamed of such happiness as this, while I was an ugly duckling."**

## Key objects and props

- **The unusually large egg** that takes longer to hatch than the others.
- **The burdock leaves** under which the mother duck nests.
- **The hunters' guns and dogs** on the moor.
- **The cat and hen** in the cottage — the two tests of "what can you do?"
- **The frozen ice** that traps the duckling.
- **The milk, butter, and flour** the duckling crashes into at the peasant's house.
- **The three swans** in the garden in spring.
- **The reflection in the water** — the moment of recognition.

## Themes and ideas

- **Belonging.** The duckling looks for a home everywhere — farmyard, marsh, cottage, peasant's kitchen — and only finds it where his real family is.
- **Being judged on appearance.** Almost every animal mocks or attacks him because of how he looks.
- **Survival and patience.** He keeps going through cruel weather and cruel words. The story rewards endurance.
- **Identity.** The duckling never changes who he is. The world changes how it sees him.
- **Hope after hardship.** The ending makes the long, sad middle of the story worth it.

## Tone and style

A traditional fairy tale told in slow, simple prose. There is real cruelty (pecking, kicking, gunfire) and real hardship (the frozen pond), but the language stays calm and storybook-like throughout. The retelling for young children softens some details but keeps the same shape: summer hatching, a long lonely journey through autumn and winter, a triumphant spring. Illustrations are detailed nature paintings of ducks, swans, marsh birds, and farmyard life.
