# Owl Moon

**Author:** Jane Yolen
**Illustrator:** John Schoenherr
**First published:** 1987
**Grade band:** Grade 2 / Ages 4–8
**Format:** Illustrated picture book (Caldecott Medal winner)

---

## Premise

A quiet, lyrical picture book about a child who goes **owling** with **Pa** late at night in the snowy woods. They walk out into the cold and the moonlight, listening for an owl. Pa calls. They wait. They walk further. Pa calls again. Finally an owl answers, and they have a long, silent meeting under the moon before the owl flies away and they walk home. The book is about patience, hope, and the quiet bond between parent and child.

## Setting

A snowy winter night in the woods near a farm. It is **late one winter night, long past bedtime**. There is **no wind**. The **trees stand still as giant statues** and **the moon is so bright the sky seems to shine**. The walk takes the child and Pa across a cornfield (white with snow), past a stand of dark trees, into a clearing, and finally to a tall tree where the owl appears.

## Characters

- **The child narrator** — a small child, telling the story in first person. The child's name and gender are not given in the text. The child has been waiting **a long, long time** for this trip.
- **Pa** — the child's father. Calm, quiet, patient. He is the one who knows how to call an owl and carries a big flashlight.
- **A great owl** — the third character, although it only appears at the end. Yellow-eyed, large, perched in a high branch.

No one else appears or is named.

## Plot in order

1. **Late at night.** The child is up long past bedtime, going out into the snow with Pa to look for an owl. The trip is called **"owling."**
2. **A still, bright night.** It is winter. There is no wind. The trees are still. The moon is bright enough to make the sky seem to shine.
3. **Quiet rules.** The first rule of owling is to **be quiet**. The child has been waiting to go owling with Pa for a long time. There is no talking on the walk.
4. **Footprints in the snow.** Their feet **crunch over the crisp snow** and they leave little grey footprints behind them. Pa makes a **long shadow** in the moonlight; the child's shadow is **short and round**. The child has to **run every now and then to keep up**.
5. **Past the cornfield and into the trees.** The two walk along, past a field and into a wood of dark trees.
6. **The first call.** Pa stops. He cups his hands around his mouth and calls: **"Whoo-whoo-who-who-who-whoooooo."** The call drifts away into the trees. Nothing answers.
7. **Walking on.** They walk further. The child listens and looks so hard that **the child's ears hurt and the child's eyes go cloudy with the cold**. Still no owl.
8. **Being brave.** The child does not ask what kinds of things hide behind the black trees in the middle of the night. **"When you go owling you have to be brave."**
9. **The clearing.** They come to a clearing where the **moon fits exactly over the centre** and the **snow is whiter than milk in a cereal bowl**.
10. **The second call.** Pa calls again: **"Whoo-whoo-who-who-who-whoooooo."** This time, far off, an owl answers.
11. **The answer comes closer.** They wait. Pa calls again, and the owl calls back, closer this time.
12. **The owl appears.** Suddenly, an **owl shadow** lifts off from the big tree shadow and flies right over the child and Pa. The owl lands in a tall tree above them.
13. **The flashlight.** Pa lifts his big **flashlight** and shines it up into the tree. The beam catches the owl. Its **yellow eyes** stare straight down at them.
14. **Looking at each other.** For one long minute, the owl and the child and Pa stand still and look at one another. Nobody talks. Nobody moves.
15. **The owl flies away.** After a while the owl lifts its wings and flies silently back into the trees.
16. **Walking home.** Now the child knows that **owling is done** — the child could talk, could even laugh out loud — but instead the child stays quiet, **a shadow walking home** beside Pa.
17. **The big idea.** The book ends with the lesson: **"When you go owling you don't need words or warm or anything but hope. That's what Pa says. The kind of hope that flies on silent wings under a shining Owl Moon."**

## Key objects and props

- **The moon** — extremely bright; the kind of moon people call an **"owl moon."**
- **The snow** — crisp and white; everything is described in terms of it.
- **Pa's big flashlight** — used to light up the owl at the climax.
- **The footprints** — small grey footprints in the snow following them through the wood.
- **The owl** — large, with **yellow eyes**, sitting high in a tree.

## Themes and ideas

- **Patience and hope.** The whole walk is a long quiet wait. The pay-off is short and silent.
- **The bond between parent and child.** Pa and the child don't speak, but they share the trip completely.
- **The rules of owling.** Be quiet. Be brave. Don't ask questions. Don't need warmth or words.
- **Nature at night.** The snow, the moon, the trees, the cold, and finally a wild owl — all painted in long, quiet sentences.
- **Things worth waiting for.** The child has waited "a long, long time." When it finally happens, it is only a minute long.

## Tone and style

Slow, hushed, poetic prose. Long sentences, many similes ("the trees stood still as giant statues," "the snow was whiter than milk in a cereal bowl," "the moon made his face into a silver mask"). John Schoenherr's watercolour illustrations are large and dim, showing the deep blue of the night and the bright white of the snow. The whole book reads at a walking pace.
