# Flat Stanley: His Original Adventure

**Author:** Jeff Brown
**Illustrator:** (multiple editions; originally Tomi Ungerer; current paperback by Macky Pamintuan)
**First published:** 1964
**Grade band:** Grade 2 / Ages 6–9
**Format:** Short illustrated chapter book

---

## Premise

A boy named **Stanley Lambchop** wakes up one morning to discover that the big **bulletin board** on his bedroom wall has fallen on him in the night and squashed him completely flat. He is now **about half an inch thick**. Instead of being a disaster, Stanley's new shape lets him do all sorts of things ordinary boys can't: slide under doors, be **mailed in an envelope** to a friend in California, fly like a **kite**, and even help catch art thieves at a museum. By the end of the book, Stanley begins to wish he were round again, and his brother **Arthur** comes up with a clever solution involving a **bicycle pump**.

## Setting

The **Lambchop family's home and bedroom** at the start. The story then expands outward: a **park** where Stanley is flown like a kite, the **post office** where he is sent through the mail, a friend's house in **California**, and a **museum** where the climax happens. The setting is a normal town in the 1960s — Stanley walks to school, his mother does the laundry, his father reads the paper.

## Characters

- **Stanley Lambchop** — the older Lambchop son. The flat boy. Cheerful, polite, and resourceful about his new shape.
- **Arthur Lambchop** — Stanley's younger brother. Sometimes jealous of all the attention Stanley gets after being flattened. He's the one who finally fixes Stanley.
- **Mr. Lambchop** — Stanley's father. Practical and unflappable. Helps Stanley figure out how to use his new flatness.
- **Mrs. Lambchop** — Stanley's mother. Equally practical; rolls Stanley up, packs him in the envelope, and helps him along.
- **Thomas Anthony Jeffrey** — Stanley's friend who lives in California, and the person Stanley visits by being mailed in an envelope.
- **The art thieves** — at the museum, two **sneak-thieves** who plan to steal a famous painting.
- **The museum's chief of police** — gives Stanley a reward after the thieves are caught.

## Plot in order

1. **A normal morning interrupted.** The Lambchop family — Mr. and Mrs. Lambchop, **Stanley**, and his younger brother **Arthur** — live a normal life. Their breakfast is interrupted when Arthur shouts that something is wrong with Stanley's bed.
2. **The big bulletin board.** During the night, the heavy **bulletin board** on Stanley's bedroom wall has fallen on top of him while he was asleep.
3. **Stanley is flat.** Stanley is unhurt, but he is now **four feet tall, about a foot wide, and half an inch thick**.
4. **The doctor's verdict.** Dr. Dan examines Stanley, says he is in perfect health, and explains that **sometimes these things just happen**. The family will need to get used to it.
5. **Advantages of being flat.** Stanley quickly discovers things he can now do:
   - He can **slide under closed doors** and into rooms when the door is locked.
   - He can **fit through narrow grates** and small spaces.
   - He can be **rolled up** when his family goes out.
6. **Mail to California.** Stanley wants to visit his friend **Thomas Anthony Jeffrey** in **California**. The trip would cost a lot of money. Mrs. Lambchop has a better idea — she **folds Stanley up, slips him into a large envelope**, and **mails him**.
7. **The trip.** Stanley travels through the post inside the envelope. Thomas Anthony Jeffrey takes him out at the other end and has a great visit with him. Eventually Stanley is mailed home again.
8. **Arthur flies him as a kite.** Back at home, Arthur takes Stanley to the **park** with a long string and **flies him as a kite**, high in the air over the trees and rooftops.
9. **Arthur gets jealous.** All this attention starts to bother Arthur, who feels overshadowed by his flat brother.
10. **Sneak thieves at the museum.** A famous painting is being stolen from the **Famous Museum** at night by **two sneak-thieves**. Stanley and his father offer to help catch them.
11. **Stanley as a painting.** Stanley dresses up as a girl in a painting (with a frame around him) and hangs himself flat on the **museum wall** as a fake picture. He stays still, pretending to be a painting on display.
12. **The catch.** That night the thieves come, and Stanley **shouts and waves his arms** from inside the frame. The thieves are so surprised that they are caught. Stanley gets his picture in the **newspaper** and a **reward** from the chief of police.
13. **Stanley feels different.** After the museum, children start teasing Stanley about being flat, and he begins to feel **sad** about not being a normal shape any more.
14. **Arthur's idea.** **Arthur** — now wanting to help — fetches a **bicycle pump**. He puts the nozzle in Stanley's mouth and **pumps him up** until Stanley is round and the proper boy-shape again.
15. **Brothers.** Stanley is overjoyed. Arthur is the hero this time. The two brothers go to bed, with the **bulletin board now safely taken down** from the wall.

## Key objects and props

- **The bulletin board** that flattens Stanley.
- **Stanley's new shape** — **four feet tall, a foot wide, half an inch thick**.
- **The big envelope** that Mrs. Lambchop mails Stanley in.
- **The kite string** that Arthur uses to fly Stanley as a kite.
- **The picture frame** Stanley hides inside at the Famous Museum.
- **The newspaper photo and reward** Stanley receives.
- **The bicycle pump** that Arthur uses to inflate Stanley again at the end.

## Themes and ideas

- **Turning a problem into an adventure.** Being flat could be a tragedy. Stanley turns it into a way to see California and catch thieves.
- **Family.** The Lambchops take Stanley's strangeness completely in stride and help him use it.
- **Sibling jealousy and reconciliation.** Arthur gets fed up with Stanley's fame, but he is the one who fixes him in the end.
- **Being yourself.** Stanley is a likeable boy whether he's flat or round.
- **Imagination.** Almost every chapter answers the question, *"If you were flat, what could you do?"*

## Tone and style

A short, friendly, funny chapter book with plenty of illustrations and not many words on the page. The sentences are short and the chapters are short too, so it works as a first chapter book. The humour is gentle. Nobody panics, nobody is mean for long, and the world treats Stanley's flatness as something cheerful and useful.
