# The Story about Ping

**Author:** Marjorie Flack
**Illustrator:** Kurt Wiese
**First published:** 1933
**Grade band:** Grade 1 / Ages 4–8
**Format:** Illustrated picture book

---

## Premise

A young domestic duck named **Ping** lives with his very large family on a houseboat on the **Yangtze River**. One evening Ping is afraid of being spanked for coming home last, so he hides — and the boat sails away without him. He spends a frightening day on the river, sees strange fishing birds, is captured by a family who plan to cook him, is saved by a small boy, and finally finds his way home — where he takes the spanking and rejoins his family.

## Setting

The **Yangtze River** in China. Most of the action takes place on or beside the water: Ping's home, the **"wise-eyed boat"** (a houseboat painted with two large eyes on the front), the riverbank, and the boat of the family that captures Ping. Time of day matters: each evening the master calls the ducks home at sunset.

## Characters

- **Ping** — a small yellow domestic duck. The main character.
- **The Master of the wise-eyed boat** — the man who owns Ping and his whole duck family. He calls them home each evening.
- **Ping's family** — his mother, his father, his **two sisters**, his **three brothers**, his **eleven aunts**, his **seven uncles**, and his **forty-two cousins**.
- **The fishing cormorants** — large dark fishing birds Ping sees during his lost day. They have **shining metal rings around their necks** that stop them swallowing the fish they catch.
- **The boat family** — a father, mother, and a small boy who capture Ping. The father plans a duck dinner. The mother plans to cook Ping with rice at sunset.
- **The little boy** — the small boy on the family's boat. He saves Ping by saying the duck is too beautiful to eat, and drops him back in the water.

## Plot in order

1. **Home on the river.** Ping lives on the **wise-eyed boat** (a houseboat with two big eyes painted on its front) on the **Yangtze River**, with his enormous family.
2. **Family count.** Ping's family includes his mother, his father, **two sisters, three brothers, eleven aunts, seven uncles, and forty-two cousins**.
3. **Daily routine.** Every morning the ducks leave the boat to feed on the riverbank. Every evening at sunset, the **Master of the wise-eyed boat** calls them home with the cry **"La-la-la-la-lei!"**
4. **The bridge and the spank.** The ducks come back over a little bridge onto the boat. The rule is that **the last duck up the bridge always gets a spank on the back**.
5. **Ping misses the call.** One evening, Ping is **wrong side up in the water trying to catch a little fish** and does not hear the master calling. By the time he looks up, the wise-eyed boat is moving down the river without him.
6. **A frightened night.** Ping hides in the grasses on the bank and **tucks his head under his wing** to sleep.
7. **Searching the river.** In the morning Ping sets off down the river to find the wise-eyed boat. He sees many kinds of boats — **big boats, little boats, fishing boats, beggar's boats, house boats, raft boats** — but none of them is his.
8. **The cormorants.** Ping sees a fisherman with **dark fishing birds (cormorants)** that have **shining metal rings tied around their necks**. The rings are so tight that the birds cannot swallow the big fish they catch — they must give them to the fisherman.
9. **Captured.** A **little boy** on a houseboat spots Ping in the water and grabs him with a **basket**. Ping is carried onto the boat as a prize.
10. **A duck dinner.** The boy's father says **"a duck dinner has come to us"** and the boy's mother says she will **cook Ping with rice at sunset**.
11. **The boy speaks up.** The little boy protests: **"My nice duck is too beautiful to eat."**
12. **Saved.** The boy carries Ping in his basket and quietly **drops him back into the water**.
13. **Home in sight.** Ping swims down the river and at last sees the **wise-eyed boat** — his home.
14. **Crossing the bridge.** Ping hurries up the bridge onto the boat. He is the last duck again, so he gets **the spank on the back**.
15. **Family again.** The book ends with Ping back **home again on the wise-eyed boat on the Yangtze River**.

## Key objects and props

- **The wise-eyed boat** — a houseboat painted with two big eyes on the front.
- **The little bridge** the ducks cross onto the boat each evening.
- **The Master's stick** — used to spank the last duck up the bridge.
- **The metal rings** around the cormorants' necks.
- **The basket** the little boy uses to carry Ping.
- **The river** — the Yangtze, busy with many kinds of boats.

## Themes and ideas

- **Belonging to a family.** Ping's very large family is the heart of the story; the spank is the price of belonging.
- **Hiding from a small punishment.** Ping tries to avoid one spank and ends up in much worse trouble — a quiet lesson about facing consequences.
- **Strangers and kindness.** A small boy chooses kindness even though his family wants duck for dinner.
- **The world is bigger than home.** Once Ping is away from the boat, the river is full of fishing boats, fishermen, strange birds, and danger.
- **Coming home.** Ping accepts the spank because being home with his family is what matters.

## Tone and style

A short, slow, gently illustrated picture-book adventure. The pictures show Chinese river life: junks and house boats, fishermen with cormorants, bamboo poles, the wise-eyed boat. Sentences are simple but the rhythm is long and rolling, suiting the river setting. The book has a small, satisfying ending where everything goes back to how it was — including the spank.
