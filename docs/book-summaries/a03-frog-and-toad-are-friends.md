# Frog and Toad Are Friends

**Author:** Arnold Lobel
**First published:** 1970
**Grade band:** Grade 1 / Ages 4–8
**Format:** Easy-reader chapter book (five short stories)

---

## Premise

Five gentle short stories about two friends — **Frog** and **Toad** — who do everything together even though they have very different temperaments. Frog is usually calm, helpful, and patient; Toad is grumpy, anxious, and easily worried. Each story is built around something simple in their friendship: waking up in spring, telling a story, losing a button, going for a swim, and waiting for the mail.

## Setting

A gentle, illustrated world of small woodland houses, porches, paths through a meadow, woods, and a river. Frog and Toad each live in their own small house, and they visit each other often. The setting is calm, rural, and out of any specific time.

## Characters

- **Frog** — the taller, greener, more cheerful of the two friends. Patient, kind, and a fixer of problems.
- **Toad** — the shorter, browner, grumpier friend. Worries, gets cross, and is the more emotional of the pair.

Other named characters that appear in the book:

- **Sparrow** — appears in *A Lost Button*; hands Toad a wrong button.
- **Raccoon** — appears in *A Lost Button*; hands Toad a wrong button.
- **Turtle, Lizards, Snake, Dragonflies, Field Mouse** — appear in *A Swim*, watching Toad from the riverbank.
- **Snail** — in *The Letter*, this is who Frog asks to carry the letter to Toad.

## The five stories, in order

### 1. Spring

- Frog wakes up on a sunny spring morning and runs to Toad's house.
- Toad is still in bed and refuses to get up. He tells Frog: **"Come back again and wake me up at about half past May."**
- Frog finds Toad's calendar and tears off the pages for **November, December, January, February, March, and April** so that Toad will think it is already May.
- Frog calls out that it is now May and Toad gets up, and they go outside together.

### 2. The Story

- Frog is not feeling well; Toad says he looks **"quite green."**
- Frog asks Toad to tell him a story to cheer him up.
- Toad tries hard to think of a story. He **walks up and down on the porch**. He **stands on his head**. He **pours a glass of water over his head**. He **bangs his head against the wall**.
- He still cannot think of one.
- Toad goes to bed feeling unwell. By then **Frog is feeling better**, and Frog tells **Toad** a story instead — the story of a frog who couldn't think of a story.

### 3. A Lost Button

- Frog and Toad go on a long walk across a **meadow**, through the **woods**, and along the **river**.
- When they get home, Toad realises a button has come off his jacket. He describes his missing button as **white, big, round, thick, and four-holed**.
- They retrace their walk to look for it. People keep finding the wrong button:
  1. **Frog** finds a **black** button. *"My button was white."*
  2. **Sparrow** brings a button with **two holes**. *"My button had four holes."*
  3. **Frog** finds a **small** button. *"My button was big."*
  4. **Raccoon** brings a **square** button. *"My button was round."*
  5. **Frog** finds a **thin** button. *"My button was thick."*
- Toad gets crosser and crosser, throws the wrong buttons up in the air, and shouts that the whole world is full of buttons but none of them is his.
- He stomps home — and finds his real **white, four-holed, big, round, thick button** lying on the **floor of his house**.
- Toad feels guilty for shouting. He **sews all of the wrong buttons onto his jacket** and gives the jacket to **Frog** as a present. Frog loves it.

### 4. A Swim

- Frog and Toad go to the **river** for a swim.
- Toad puts on a **bathing suit**. He thinks he looks **funny** in it and does not want anyone to see him.
- Toad goes into the water first and tells Frog he must not look until Toad is well covered.
- While Toad is in the water, animals arrive on the bank one by one, in this order: **Turtle, Lizards, Snake, Dragonflies, Field Mouse** — all curious to see Toad in his bathing suit.
- Toad stays in the water for a long time and gets cold.
- When he finally gets out, all of the animals — and Frog — laugh at him because he really does look funny in his bathing suit.
- Toad picks up his clothes and **goes home**.

### 5. The Letter

- Toad sits sadly on the front porch. Frog asks why. Toad says **he has never had any mail** — his mailbox is always **empty**.
- Frog runs home and writes Toad a letter. He gives it to a **snail** and asks him to take it to Toad's house.
- Frog goes back and sits on Toad's porch with him to wait. Toad still thinks he will never get a letter and refuses to look at the mailbox.
- Frog finally tells Toad that he has written him a letter, and tells Toad what the letter says: **"Dear Toad, I am glad that you are my best friend. Your best friend, Frog."**
- Toad says it is **a very good letter**.
- They sit on the porch together feeling happy.
- **Four days later** the **snail** arrives at Toad's door with the letter. Toad is very pleased to have it.

## Key objects and props

- **Toad's calendar** — Frog tears off the months November through April.
- **Toad's jacket** — loses a button; later ends up covered in wrong buttons and given to Frog.
- **The lost button** — white, big, round, thick, with four holes; eventually found on Toad's own floor.
- **Toad's bathing suit** — the source of all the embarrassment in *A Swim*.
- **The letter** — short, signed "Your best friend, Frog."
- **The mailbox** — empty until the snail finally arrives.
- **The snail** — slow but reliable letter carrier.

## Themes and ideas

- **Friendship through differences** — Frog is calm and Toad is anxious; the book shows that very different friends can love each other.
- **Kindness and patience** — Frog goes to a lot of trouble to make Toad happy (tearing off calendar pages, hunting for buttons, writing a letter, waiting four days on the porch).
- **Small feelings matter** — being grumpy in the morning, being embarrassed in a swimsuit, being sad about an empty mailbox — none of these are dismissed.
- **Saying sorry without saying sorry** — Toad's apology for his bad temper is the jacket of buttons he sews for Frog.
- **The pleasure of waiting** — even though the letter takes four days, the waiting is shown as a happy thing because the friends do it together.

## Tone and style

Calm, slow, slightly funny prose. Sentences are short and the same shapes repeat, which makes the book very easy to read aloud or to read alone as a first chapter book. Soft watercolour illustrations of Frog (green, jacket and trousers) and Toad (brown, jacket and trousers). The humour is in the small, recognisably human troubles of the two friends.
