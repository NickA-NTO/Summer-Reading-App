# The Enormous Turnip

**Author:** Traditional Russian folktale, retold for Usborne
**Original publication:** Collected by Alexander Afanasyev in 1863 as *Repka*
**Grade band:** Grade K / Ages 4–6
**Format:** Illustrated retelling for early readers (Usborne)

---

## Premise

A simple cumulative story from Russia. An **old man** plants a **turnip seed** in his garden. The turnip grows and grows and grows until it is **enormous**. When it is time to pull it up, the old man cannot do it alone. He calls the **old woman** to help. She isn't strong enough either, so she calls the **granddaughter**. They still cannot pull it up, so they call the **dog**. Then the **cat**. Finally a tiny **mouse** joins on at the end of the line — and with the mouse's help, **out comes the enormous turnip at last**.

## Setting

A small farm in the countryside. An old man and old woman's vegetable garden, where one turnip has grown to a huge size. The story takes place over the time the turnip grows, then on the day everyone pulls it up.

## Characters

- **The old man** — plants the turnip and starts the pulling.
- **The old woman** — the old man's wife.
- **The granddaughter** — the girl who lives with them.
- **The dog** — the family dog.
- **The cat** — the family cat.
- **The mouse** — the smallest helper, but the one whose help finally does it.

## Plot in order

1. **The seed.** The **old man** plants a small **turnip seed** in his garden.
2. **It grows enormous.** The turnip grows… and grows… and grows… until it is **enormous** — far bigger than any turnip ever before.
3. **The old man tries.** The old man takes hold of the turnip and **pulls and pulls**. But it will not come out of the ground. The turnip is too big for him alone.
4. **He calls the old woman.** The old man calls his wife. **The old woman holds the old man, the old man holds the turnip**, and they pull together. Still nothing.
5. **The granddaughter.** They call their **granddaughter**. **The granddaughter holds the old woman, the old woman holds the old man, the old man holds the turnip.** They all pull together. Still nothing.
6. **The dog.** They call the **dog**. The dog holds the granddaughter, the granddaughter holds the old woman, the old woman holds the old man, the old man holds the turnip. They pull and pull. Still nothing.
7. **The cat.** They call the **cat**. The cat holds the dog, the dog holds the granddaughter, the granddaughter holds the old woman, the old woman holds the old man, the old man holds the turnip. They pull. Still it will not come up.
8. **The mouse.** Finally the cat calls the tiny **mouse**. The mouse holds the cat, the cat holds the dog, the dog holds the granddaughter, the granddaughter holds the old woman, the old woman holds the old man, the old man holds the turnip.
9. **Out it comes.** They all pull together — and with the mouse's tiny extra strength, the **enormous turnip suddenly pops out of the ground**.
10. **Everybody falls.** They all topple over backwards in a heap.
11. **A turnip feast.** Together, they pull the enormous turnip home and have a great meal.

## Key objects and props

- **The turnip seed** the old man plants.
- **The enormous turnip** — the centre of the whole story.
- **The line of helpers**: old man, old woman, granddaughter, dog, cat, mouse.

## Themes and ideas

- **Teamwork.** No one could pull the turnip up alone — together, they could.
- **Everyone matters.** Even the smallest helper (the mouse) makes the difference.
- **A cumulative tale.** The same pattern repeats, getting longer each time, until the very end.
- **Family and home.** A grandfather, grandmother, and granddaughter living together with their pets.
- **Patience and perseverance.** They didn't give up; they kept calling for one more helper.

## Tone and style

A short, repeating fairy-tale retelling. Each spread adds one more helper to the line. The same line — *"They pulled and pulled and pulled, but the turnip would not come up"* — is repeated again and again, so children can join in. Bright, simple illustrations show the line of pullers growing longer.
