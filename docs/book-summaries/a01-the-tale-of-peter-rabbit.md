# The Tale of Peter Rabbit

**Author:** Beatrix Potter
**First published:** 1902
**Grade band:** Grade 1 / Ages 4–7
**Format:** Short illustrated story

---

## Premise

A small rabbit named Peter ignores his mother's warning and sneaks into a gardener's forbidden garden, where he eats too many vegetables, is chased, loses his clothes, and only just escapes home — ending up sick in bed while his obedient sisters enjoy a proper supper.

## Setting

The countryside. The rabbits live in **a sand-bank, underneath the root of a very big fir tree**. Most of the story takes place in **Mr. McGregor's garden**, which sits over the wall from the rabbits' home. The garden contains rows of vegetables, a cucumber frame, gooseberry nets, a tool shed, and a gate.

## Characters

- **Peter Rabbit** — the naughty young rabbit. He wears a **blue jacket with brass buttons** (described as "quite new") and a pair of **shoes**.
- **Flopsy, Mopsy, and Cotton-tail** — Peter's three obedient sisters.
- **Mrs. Rabbit** — the mother. She warns the children and looks after Peter when he gets home.
- **Peter's father** — only mentioned. He had "an accident" in Mr. McGregor's garden and was **put in a pie by Mrs. McGregor**.
- **Mr. McGregor** — the gardener; the rabbits' enemy. Chases Peter through the garden.
- **Mrs. McGregor** — Mr. McGregor's wife; mentioned only as the one who put Peter's father in a pie.
- **Friendly sparrows** — small birds who fly down to Peter when he is stuck in the net and **implore him to exert himself**.
- **A white cat** — Peter sees it staring at some goldfish in a pond. He chooses to walk away without speaking to it.

## Plot in order

1. **The warning.** Mrs. Rabbit tells Flopsy, Mopsy, Cotton-tail, and Peter that they may go into the fields or down the lane, but **not into Mr. McGregor's garden**, because their father had an accident there and was put in a pie by Mrs. McGregor.
2. **Mother leaves.** Mrs. Rabbit goes through the wood to the baker's and buys **a loaf of brown bread and five currant buns**.
3. **The obedient sisters.** Flopsy, Mopsy, and Cotton-tail go off to gather **blackberries**.
4. **Peter disobeys.** Peter runs straight to Mr. McGregor's garden and squeezes under the gate.
5. **The feast.** Peter eats **lettuces**, then **French beans**, then **radishes**. Feeling sick, he goes looking for **parsley**.
6. **The encounter.** Round the end of a **cucumber frame** he comes face to face with Mr. McGregor, who jumps up and chases him, shouting **"Stop thief!"**
7. **The shoes.** Running in fright, Peter loses **one shoe among the cabbages** and **the other shoe amongst the potatoes**.
8. **Caught in the net.** Peter gets stuck in a **gooseberry net** when the **large buttons** on his jacket catch.
9. **The sparrows.** Friendly sparrows fly to him and beg him to **exert himself**.
10. **The sieve.** Mr. McGregor comes with a **sieve**, which he means to pop on top of Peter to catch him. Peter wriggles out just in time, leaving his jacket behind.
11. **The watering can.** Peter rushes into a **tool shed** and jumps into a **can** — but the can has **water** in it.
12. **The sneeze.** Peter gives himself away with a sneeze — **"Kertyschoo!"** — and Mr. McGregor begins searching the shed.
13. **Out the window.** Peter jumps from a window, upsetting **three plants**. Mr. McGregor cannot fit through, gets tired, and goes back to his work.
14. **Lost and wet.** Peter sits down to rest. He is out of breath, trembling with fright, soaked from the watering can, and does not know which way to go.
15. **The cat.** He sees a **white cat** staring at some **goldfish** by a pond and decides to walk away without speaking to it.
16. **The wheelbarrow.** Peter climbs onto a **wheelbarrow** and from there spots Mr. McGregor in the distance and, beyond him, **the gate**.
17. **The escape.** Peter runs as fast as he can and **slips underneath the gate** to safety.
18. **The scarecrow.** Mr. McGregor hangs the lost **jacket and shoes** on a **scare-crow** to frighten the **blackbirds**.
19. **Home again.** Peter runs home and flops down on the floor of the rabbit-hole. His mother wonders what he has done with his clothes — it is the **second little jacket and pair of shoes** Peter has lost in a **fortnight**.
20. **Sick in bed.** Peter is not well in the evening. Mrs. Rabbit puts him to bed and gives him **camomile tea** — **one table-spoonful at bed-time**.
21. **Supper for the good rabbits.** Flopsy, Mopsy, and Cotton-tail have **bread and milk and blackberries** for supper.

## Key objects and props

- **Peter's blue jacket** with **brass buttons** ("quite new") — caught in the gooseberry net; ends up on the scarecrow.
- **Peter's shoes** — one lost in the cabbages, one in the potatoes.
- **A loaf of brown bread and five currant buns** — what Mrs. Rabbit buys at the baker's.
- **A cucumber frame** — where Peter and Mr. McGregor first meet.
- **A gooseberry net** — where Peter gets stuck.
- **A sieve** — Mr. McGregor's tool for catching Peter.
- **A watering can** — Peter's hiding place; it has water in it.
- **A wheelbarrow** — Peter climbs on it to see the gate.
- **A scare-crow** — dressed in Peter's jacket and shoes to frighten blackbirds.
- **Camomile tea** — Peter's bedtime medicine, one tablespoonful.

## Themes and ideas

- **Obedience versus disobedience** — Peter ignores his mother's warning and pays for it.
- **Curiosity and temptation** — the forbidden garden is too inviting to resist.
- **Consequences** — Peter loses his new clothes, gets soaked, gets sick, and misses out on a nice supper.
- **Family contrast** — the obedient sisters get treats; the naughty brother ends up in bed with medicine.
- **Quick thinking and a bit of luck** — Peter does escape, helped by the sparrows and his own scrambling.

## Tone and style

Short, gently humorous prose with classic watercolour illustrations of rabbits in human clothes. The narrator addresses the reader directly and uses some old-fashioned vocabulary (**fortnight**, **table-spoonful**, **camomile**, **gooseberry net**, **currant buns**). The whole story reads in one sitting.
