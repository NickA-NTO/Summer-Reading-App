# The Boxcar Children

**Author:** Gertrude Chandler Warner
**Illustrator:** L. Kate Deal
**First published:** 1924 (rewritten and republished 1942)
**Grade band:** Grade 3 / Ages 7–10
**Format:** Children's novel (first book in the long-running Boxcar Children series)

---

## Premise

Four young orphaned siblings — **Henry, Jessie, Violet, and Benny Alden** — are afraid that the grandfather they've never met will be cruel, and that they will be split up. So they run away. After sleeping in the woods, they discover an **abandoned boxcar** in a forest clearing and turn it into a snug home — finding dishes, a stream for water, blueberries to eat, and even a stray dog they name **Watch**. **Henry** gets work as the doctor's helper in the nearest village. When **Violet** falls seriously ill, the children meet the kind **Dr. Moore**, who realises who they are. He brings their **grandfather** to them, and to their surprise he turns out to be a warm, rich, kind man. The children go to live with him — and he has the boxcar moved into his garden as their playhouse.

## Setting

The American countryside in the 1920s/40s. The book moves through:

- **A small town** with a bakery, where the children first try to hide.
- **The woods** outside town, where they sleep on pine needles.
- **A clearing in the woods** with an **abandoned boxcar** beside a stream.
- **The village of Silver City** (the local town nearby), where Henry works.
- **Dr. Moore's house**, where Violet is taken when she falls ill.
- **Grandfather James Alden's big house**, where the children finally live.

## Characters

- **Henry James Alden** — the oldest child, **fourteen** years old. Hardworking, sensible, and the family's "father figure" while they are on the run. Gets a job in town to support the family.
- **Jessie Alden** — twelve years old. The "mother" of the four. Keeps house in the boxcar, makes meals, decorates with flowers, looks after everyone.
- **Violet Alden** — ten years old. Gentle, kind, artistic. She is the one who falls seriously ill.
- **Benny Alden** — six years old. The youngest. Cheerful, curious, loves his stuffed animal "Cinnamon Bear" and is very fond of the dog.
- **Watch** — a stray dog the children adopt. They name him Watch because they hope he will watch their boxcar at night. He becomes their loyal friend.
- **Dr. Moore** — the kind village doctor. Hires Henry for small jobs, secretly looks out for the children, and is the one who finally reunites them with their grandfather.
- **James Henry Alden** ("Grandfather Alden") — the children's wealthy grandfather. The whole story they have been hiding from him because they thought he was cruel. He turns out to be **kind, wealthy, and loving**.

## Plot in order

1. **Four orphans.** Henry, Jessie, Violet, and Benny Alden have lost their parents. They are alone in the world.
2. **The grandfather they fear.** The children have heard their grandfather, **James Henry Alden**, is unkind, and that he never approved of their parents' marriage. They are sure he will be cruel to them.
3. **At the bakery.** One warm night, the children stop at a small **bakery** and ask if they can rest. The baker's wife agrees — but the children overhear her saying she will keep three of them but send **Benny** away, separating the family.
4. **Into the woods.** Rather than be split up, they run away in the dark. They sleep on **pine needles** under the trees.
5. **A storm.** The next day a storm threatens. **Jessie** searches for shelter and finds an abandoned **boxcar** in a clearing.
6. **A home.** The children decide the boxcar will be their home. They explore the area and find a **stream** for water, a **junkyard** with useful pots and dishes, and **blueberry bushes** to eat from.
7. **Setting up house.** **Jessie** sweeps the boxcar out and decorates it with flowers. **Violet** arranges everyone's beds with pine needles. **Henry** builds a small fireplace nearby for cooking. **Benny** finds a pink cup at the dump that becomes his.
8. **Watch the dog.** A friendly stray dog wanders up to the boxcar. Jessie spots a **thorn in his paw** and gently removes it. The dog stays. They call him **Watch**.
9. **Henry goes to work.** Henry walks into the nearest village, **Silver City**, to look for work. He meets the kind **Dr. Moore**, who hires him for odd jobs around his house and garden, paying him with money and **food and clothes** to take back to the family.
10. **Henry races.** At the town's **Field Day**, Henry enters the **running race** and wins — using the name "Henry James." Dr. Moore notices the unusual name and begins to wonder.
11. **Violet falls ill.** **Violet** becomes seriously sick — feverish and weak. Henry runs to fetch Dr. Moore.
12. **Found out.** Dr. Moore brings all four children to his own home so he can care for Violet properly. While they are there, he quietly works out who they really are.
13. **The grandfather appears.** Dr. Moore sends word to **Mr. Alden** that his missing grandchildren are safe. Mr. Alden hurries to Dr. Moore's house.
14. **A pleasant surprise.** The children meet their grandfather for the first time and discover he is **not cruel at all** — he is a warm, gentle, **wealthy** man who has been searching for them.
15. **A new home.** The four children go to live in their grandfather's big house. They are loved and well looked after.
16. **The boxcar saved.** Mr. Alden has their old **boxcar** lifted out of the woods, **repainted**, and set up in **his garden** as a **playhouse** so the children never have to lose it.

## Key objects and props

- **The abandoned boxcar** in the woods — the children's first real home together.
- **Benny's pink cup**, found at the junk pile.
- **The stream** that supplies water and Henry uses for laundry.
- **The blueberries** the children pick for food.
- **Watch's thorn** — pulled from his paw the day they meet.
- **Henry's wages** from Dr. Moore — what keeps the family fed.
- **The footrace at Field Day** — where Dr. Moore sees Henry running and notices his name.
- **Violet's fever** — the crisis that brings the doctor and grandfather into the children's lives.
- **The boxcar in Grandfather's garden** at the end — restored as a play-house.

## Themes and ideas

- **Brothers and sisters as a family.** Henry, Jessie, Violet, and Benny are everything to one another.
- **Making do and making home.** The children turn a rusty boxcar into something snug and beautiful. Resourcefulness is the heart of the book.
- **Misjudging people.** The grandfather is not the villain the children think he is. The book gently warns against believing rumours.
- **Kindness from strangers.** Dr. Moore quietly helps the children long before they know who he is.
- **Honest work.** Henry earning the food the children eat is shown as a good and dignified thing.

## Tone and style

A gentle, straightforward children's novel. Sentences are short and easy. There is some sadness (orphans, illness, hunger) but the tone is hopeful from beginning to end. Many small domestic scenes — sweeping the boxcar, picking blueberries, washing clothes — fill the middle of the book. Black-and-white illustrations scatter through the pages.
