# The Gruffalo

**Author:** Julia Donaldson
**Illustrator:** Axel Scheffler
**First published:** 1999
**Grade band:** Grade K / Ages 3–7
**Format:** Rhyming illustrated picture book

---

## Premise

A small clever **mouse** takes a walk through the **deep dark wood** and meets, in turn, a hungry **fox**, an **owl**, and a **snake**. Each one invites him home to be eaten. The mouse escapes each time by claiming he is on his way to meet a terrifying creature called the **Gruffalo**, and inventing a more horrifying detail about him every time. Each predator runs away. Then the mouse runs into a **real** gruffalo — exactly like the one he made up. To save himself again, the mouse claims **he** is the **scariest creature in the wood** and walks back through the forest with the gruffalo at his heels. The other animals, terrified of the gruffalo, all flee. The gruffalo, thinking they are afraid of the mouse, panics and runs off too — leaving the mouse free to enjoy his **nut** in peace.

## Setting

The **deep dark wood**. The story moves through three of the predators' homes:

- **Fox's underground house**.
- **Owl's treetop house**.
- **Snake's logpile house**.

And then back through the wood, with the real gruffalo trailing along behind the mouse.

## Characters

- **The mouse** — small, brown, and very clever. The hero.
- **The fox** — sleek, hungry, wants to make the mouse into lunch.
- **The owl** — wants to make the mouse into tea.
- **The snake** — wants to make the mouse into a feast.
- **The Gruffalo** — a large brown monster with **terrible tusks, terrible claws, terrible teeth in terrible jaws, knobbly knees, turned-out toes, a poisonous wart on the end of his nose, orange eyes, a black tongue, and purple prickles all over his back**. Real, but not very clever.

## Plot in order

1. **A walk in the wood.** A **mouse** takes a stroll through the **deep dark wood**.
2. **The fox.** A **fox** appears and admires how tasty the mouse looks. The fox invites him for **lunch in his underground house**.
3. **A made-up friend.** The mouse politely says no — he is meeting a **gruffalo**. The fox has never heard of a gruffalo. The mouse describes him: **terrible tusks, terrible claws, terrible teeth in his terrible jaws**. The mouse adds that the gruffalo's favourite food is **roasted fox**. The fox **flees**.
4. **The owl.** Further on, an **owl** sees the mouse and invites him for **tea in his treetop house**. The mouse again says he is meeting a gruffalo and describes more features: **knobbly knees**, **turned-out toes**, and **a poisonous wart on the end of his nose**. The gruffalo's favourite food, the mouse adds, is **owl ice cream**. The owl **flees**.
5. **The snake.** A **snake** rises up and invites the mouse for a **feast at his logpile house**. The mouse again refuses — he is meeting a gruffalo. He adds the last features: the gruffalo's **eyes are orange**, his **tongue is black**, and he has **purple prickles all over his back**. The gruffalo's favourite food is **scrambled snake**. The snake **flees**.
6. **Pleased with himself.** The mouse laughs and says to himself that there is no such thing as a gruffalo.
7. **A real gruffalo.** And then he meets one. A **real Gruffalo** stands in front of him with all the features he invented — tusks, claws, terrible teeth, knobbly knees, turned-out toes, poisonous wart, orange eyes, black tongue, **purple prickles** and all.
8. **In trouble.** The Gruffalo announces that his favourite food is **roasted mouse**.
9. **Quick thinking.** The mouse insists the Gruffalo follow him through the wood. He claims **HE** is the **scariest creature in the wood**. The Gruffalo doesn't believe it, but follows the mouse to see.
10. **Back past the snake.** As the two of them walk past the **logpile house**, the snake — terrified of the gruffalo — turns and runs. The Gruffalo thinks the snake has run from **the mouse**.
11. **Back past the owl.** They walk past the **treetop house** and the owl bolts away. Again the Gruffalo thinks the owl is running from the mouse.
12. **Back past the fox.** They walk past the **underground house** and the fox flees too. Now the Gruffalo is sure: this little mouse really IS the scariest thing in the wood.
13. **A last bluff.** The mouse tells the Gruffalo: "My favourite food is **gruffalo crumble**." Terrified, the **Gruffalo runs away**.
14. **A peaceful snack.** With the wood now empty of predators, the mouse finds his **nut** and **sits down to eat it** quietly.

## Key objects and props

- **The deep dark wood** — the setting of the whole story.
- **Fox's underground house**, **Owl's treetop house**, **Snake's logpile house** — the three invitations.
- **The Gruffalo's features**, listed in three sets:
  - **tusks, claws, teeth and jaws**,
  - **knobbly knees, turned-out toes, poisonous wart**,
  - **orange eyes, black tongue, purple prickles**.
- **Three fake "favourite foods"** for the made-up gruffalo: **roasted fox, owl ice cream, scrambled snake**.
- **"Gruffalo crumble"** — the favourite food of the mouse, used to scare the real Gruffalo.
- **The mouse's nut** at the end.

## Themes and ideas

- **Brains over brawn.** The smallest creature in the wood outwits every bigger one.
- **The power of imagination.** The mouse invents the Gruffalo and then has to live with it when the Gruffalo turns out to be real.
- **The same trick, twice.** The mouse uses the same strategy on the Gruffalo that he used on the fox, owl, and snake.
- **Bluffing.** Each predator is too scared to question whether the mouse is telling the truth.
- **Earning a quiet snack.** The little mouse just wants to eat his nut in peace, and at the end he does.

## Tone and style

Bouncy rhyming couplets throughout, with repeating phrases ("**A gruffalo? What's a gruffalo?**", "**Silly old fox! Doesn't he know? There's no such thing as a gruffaloooo!**") that children quickly memorise and read along to. Axel Scheffler's warm, slightly sinister cartoon illustrations show three different predators and one big brown monster against a deep green wood.
