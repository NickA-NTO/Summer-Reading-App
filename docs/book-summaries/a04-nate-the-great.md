# Nate the Great

**Author:** Marjorie Weinman Sharmat
**Illustrator:** Marc Simont
**First published:** 1972
**Grade band:** Grade 1 / Ages 6–8
**Format:** Early chapter book / beginning mystery

---

## Premise

A young boy who calls himself **Nate the Great** is a self-styled detective. In this first book of the series, his friend **Annie** loses a picture she has painted, and Nate takes the case. He questions everyone who has seen the picture, follows his clues, and — between pancake breaks — works out what happened to it.

## Setting

The neighbourhood where Nate, Annie, and their friend Rosamond all live. Nate eats breakfast at his own house and walks over to Annie's, then to Rosamond's, then back to Annie's. The story moves between three houses and the yard outside Annie's. There are no exact place names — just "my house," "Annie's house," "Rosamond's house."

## Characters

- **Nate the Great** — the boy detective and narrator. He talks in short, serious, hard-boiled sentences, as if he were a grown-up detective. He **works alone**. He loves pancakes.
- **Annie** — Nate's friend. She has painted a picture and lost it. Her whole bedroom is **yellow**, and she likes the colour yellow.
- **Fang** — Annie's **big yellow dog**. He buries things (including bones) in the yard.
- **Rosamond** — Annie's friend, described as **strange**. She has four cats.
- **The four cats** — all called **Hex**. Their full names are **Super Hex, Big Hex, Plain Hex, and Little Hex**.
- **Harry** — Annie's little brother. He has been painting his own pictures using **red paint**.

## Plot in order

1. **Breakfast.** Nate is eating breakfast at home. He has "pancakes, juice, pancakes, milk, and pancakes." He likes pancakes.
2. **The phone call.** Annie phones Nate for help. She has lost a picture she painted.
3. **The case begins.** Nate walks over to Annie's. As he goes, he announces to the reader: **"Now I, Nate the Great, will find a lost picture."**
4. **At Annie's house.** Annie tells Nate the picture is of her dog **Fang**. She painted it yesterday and now cannot find it anywhere.
5. **The yellow room.** Nate asks to see Annie's room. The room is **big**, with a **yellow bed, a yellow chair, and a yellow desk** — and yellow walls. Nate concludes: **Annie likes yellow.**
6. **Suspect: Fang.** Nate notices Fang is a **big yellow dog** who **buries things in the yard**. He wonders if Fang has buried the picture.
7. **Digging.** Nate and Annie dig up the yard. They find buried bones, but no picture.
8. **Suspect: Rosamond.** Annie says her friend Rosamond saw the picture too. Nate and Annie go to Rosamond's house.
9. **Rosamond is strange.** Rosamond has **four cats**, all of them with "Hex" in their names: **Super Hex, Big Hex, Plain Hex, and Little Hex**. Nate decides Rosamond is strange but did not take the picture.
10. **Suspect: Harry.** Back at Annie's, Nate questions her **little brother Harry**. Harry has been painting his own pictures with **red paint** — including, in one of them, what he calls a **red monster**.
11. **The solution.** Nate looks at Harry's painting and realises what has happened. **Yellow and red make orange.** Harry has painted on top of Annie's **yellow** picture of Fang with **red** paint, so the picture is now an **orange monster**.
12. **Case closed.** Annie is upset at first that her picture has been painted over, but she **forgives Harry**. Nate has solved another case.

## Key objects and props

- **Pancakes** — Nate's signature food. He eats them at the start and is offered more by Annie.
- **The lost picture** — painted in yellow by Annie; a picture of her dog Fang.
- **Harry's red paint** — the cause of the mystery.
- **The "orange monster" painting** — Harry's painted-over version, which solves the case.
- **Annie's yellow room** — the clue that Annie paints in yellow.
- **The yard** — where Fang buries bones, dug up looking for the picture.

## Themes and ideas

- **Being a detective.** Nate's voice is the joke of the book — a child speaking like a serious, lonely grown-up detective.
- **Logic and clues.** The mystery is solved by looking at the evidence (yellow room, red paint, orange picture) and putting it together.
- **Colours and mixing.** The whole solution rests on **yellow + red = orange**, which young readers can work out themselves.
- **Family and friends.** Annie forgives her little brother. Nate helps a friend.
- **Routine and self-importance.** Nate keeps stopping for pancakes and keeps reminding everyone (and himself) that he is "Nate the Great."

## Tone and style

Short, dry, declarative sentences in first person. Each sentence often takes its own line. The narrator constantly refers to himself in the third person as **"I, Nate the Great,"** which is the running joke. The book is structured like a detective novel — opening case, suspects, clues, breakthrough — but the case is a missing picture and the detective is a small boy with a deerstalker-style hat who keeps stopping for pancakes.
