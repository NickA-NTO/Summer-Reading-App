# The Princess and the Pea

**Author:** Hans Christian Andersen (this Usborne edition illustrated by Dorothée Duntze)
**First published:** 1835
**Grade band:** Grade 1 / Ages 5–7
**Format:** Illustrated retelling for early readers

---

## Premise

A **prince** searches the world for a **real princess** to marry, but every princess he meets seems somehow not quite right. One terrible stormy night, a soaking-wet young woman knocks on the palace gate and says she is a real princess. To find out if she is telling the truth, the **queen** invents a clever test: she puts a single **pea** at the bottom of an enormous pile of **twenty mattresses and twenty featherbeds**, and gives the visitor that bed for the night. In the morning the young woman complains that **she barely slept a wink** because something hard in the bed had **bruised her black and blue**. Only a **real princess** could be so delicate. The prince happily marries her, and the pea is put on display in a museum.

## Setting

A castle on a stormy night. The story moves between the **palace** (where the prince lives with his royal parents), the **gate** where the princess arrives, and the **guest bedroom** where the test is set up. Time is one stormy night and the morning after.

## Characters

- **The prince** — looking everywhere for a true princess to marry.
- **The king** — the prince's father.
- **The queen** — the prince's mother. Wise and a little suspicious. The one who designs the test.
- **The young woman / the princess** — arrives at the gate soaked through. Claims to be a real princess. Tested by the queen.

## Plot in order

1. **The prince's search.** A prince wants to marry a real princess. He travels far and wide. He meets many princesses, but **something is never quite right** about any of them. He comes home unhappy.
2. **A stormy night.** One night a terrible storm rolls in, with **thunder, lightning, and pouring rain**.
3. **A knock at the gate.** Above the noise of the storm comes a knock on the castle gate. The old king goes to answer it.
4. **A soaking guest.** Outside stands a **young woman**. Her hair, clothes and shoes are **dripping wet**. Water is running out of her shoes. She says she is a **princess**.
5. **The queen is doubtful.** The queen does not believe she really is a princess — she does not look much like one in such a state. So the queen secretly plans a test.
6. **The bed.** The queen goes to the guest bedroom. At the bottom of the bed, on the wooden frame, she places **a single pea**.
7. **Twenty mattresses, twenty featherbeds.** On top of the pea, she lays **twenty mattresses**. On top of those, she lays **twenty soft featherbeds**. The bed is now towering above the floor.
8. **The princess goes to bed.** The young woman is shown to this huge bed for the night.
9. **In the morning.** The royal family asks her at breakfast how she slept. She says, **terribly**. There was **something hard** in the bed that hurt her all night long. She is **black and blue all over**.
10. **The proof.** Only a **real princess** could be so delicate that she would feel a single pea right through twenty mattresses and twenty featherbeds. The queen is delighted: this is a real princess after all.
11. **A wedding.** The prince, who has been looking for a real princess all this time, is overjoyed. He **marries her**.
12. **The pea in a museum.** The pea is **placed in a museum**, where (according to the story) it may still be seen today — unless someone has taken it.

## Key objects and props

- **The storm** that brings the princess to the castle.
- **The castle gate** where she knocks, dripping wet.
- **The pea** placed at the bottom of the bed.
- **Twenty mattresses and twenty featherbeds** piled on top of the pea.
- **The royal museum** where the pea is later kept.

## Themes and ideas

- **What makes a "real" princess?** The story plays gently with the idea — "real" means delicate, refined, easily bothered by a tiny inconvenience.
- **Looking past appearances.** A soaking-wet visitor turns out to be exactly what the prince was looking for.
- **A clever test.** The queen finds out the truth without anyone arguing.
- **Coming home.** After a long, sad search, the prince finds what he wanted at his own front door.
- **A funny twist.** The story ends with a museum item that is almost a joke at its own expense.

## Tone and style

A very short fairy tale — Hans Christian Andersen's original is barely a page long. The Usborne retelling keeps it short and sweet, with rich illustrations of the lightning-lit castle, the dripping princess, and the towering bed. The tone is gently witty rather than dramatic.
