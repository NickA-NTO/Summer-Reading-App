# Hop on Pop

**Author and Illustrator:** Dr. Seuss (Theodor Seuss Geisel)
**First published:** 1963
**Subtitle:** *The Simplest Seuss for Youngest Use*
**Grade band:** Pre-K / Earliest beginning reader
**Format:** Rhyming picture book (Beginner Books series)

---

## Premise

A very short rhyming picture book made up of dozens of tiny rhyming scenes designed for the **youngest possible readers**. Each spread shows one rhyming pair (and often a third matching word) — *Up / Pup*, *Red / Bed*, *Pat / Sat*, *Mouse / House*, *Day / Play*, *Hop / Pop* — with a small cartoon scene to match. Some pairs become little stories: Pat tries to sit on different things, Mr. Brown bumps into Mr. Black, three children try to hop on **Pop** and Pop tells them firmly to **stop**. There is almost no plot — the book is really an introduction to phonics and word families that a child can read for themselves.

## Setting

A bright, made-up cartoon world of small Seuss creatures, houses, hills, trees, and a few human-shaped characters. There is no single setting — each rhyming pair has its own little scene.

## Characters (the named ones who show up in the rhymes)

- **Pop** — the father figure. The children want to hop on him; he tells them not to.
- **Pat** — a small character who keeps sitting on things (a hat, a cat, a bat) and being told he must not sit on the cactus.
- **Red, Ned, Ted, and Ed** — four small characters in a bed together.
- **Will** — a small character who goes up a hill.
- **Jim** — used in the *Him / Jim* rhyme.
- **Mr. Brown** and **Mr. Black** — two characters who meet up.
- **Pup** and a few other small animals (a mouse, a house, a cat, a bat).

## What happens / what's in the book

The book is built out of dozens of tiny rhyming pages. Some of the most famous and most easily quizzable ones:

- **UP PUP** — Pup is up.
- **CUP / PUP** — Pup is in cup, cup is on pup.
- **MOUSE / HOUSE** — Mouse on house, house on mouse.
- **ALL / TALL / SMALL / BALL / WALL / FALL** — variations on "all": all are tall, all are small, all play ball, ball on wall, all fall.
- **PAT / SAT** — Pat sat on a **hat**, Pat sat on a **cat**, Pat sat on a **bat** — and the book warns **"Pat must not sit on that!"** when it shows a **cactus**.
- **RED / BED** — **Red, Ned, Ted, and Ed** are all squeezed into a **bed** together.
- **SAD / DAD / BAD / HAD** — Dad is sad — very, very sad — because he had a bad day.
- **DAY / PLAY** — they play all day.
- **NIGHT / FIGHT** — they fight all night.
- **HE / ME** / **HIM / JIM** — small character interactions.
- **WILL / HILL** — Will is up a hill (and stays still).
- **BROWN / DOWN** — Mr. Brown is upside down.
- **BROWN / BLACK** — Mr. Brown and Mr. Black come together for a snack.
- **HOP / POP** — Three children declare **"We like to hop. We like to hop on top of Pop."** Pop responds firmly: **"STOP! You must not hop on Pop!"**
- **CONSTANTINOPLE / TIMBUKTU** — a moment when the father is shown reading "big words like **Constantinople** and **Timbuktu**" to show that little readers can graduate to big words too.

## Key objects and props

- **The bed** with four small characters (Red, Ned, Ted, Ed) in it.
- **The hat, cat, bat,** and **cactus** that Pat does (and does not) sit on.
- **Pop** — being hopped on by the children.
- **The hill** Will climbs.
- **The cup and the pup**.

## Themes and ideas

- **Phonics and rhyme.** The whole book is a rhyme exercise.
- **Word families.** Children see groups like *cat / hat / bat / sat* and learn to read them.
- **Tiny stories from tiny words.** Each rhyme is also a quick scene that the reader can picture.
- **A child can read this themselves.** The whole point is that the words are simple enough for the youngest readers to read alone.
- **Big words too.** "Constantinople and Timbuktu" hint that big words are coming — just not yet.

## Tone and style

Two- or three-word lines on each page, often just a single rhyming pair. Bright, simple cartoon illustrations. The book is meant to be read aloud (and read along with) by a child who is just learning to recognise words. There is no plot in the usual sense — it's a tour through tiny rhymes.
