# The True Story of the Three Little Pigs

**Author:** Jon Scieszka
**Illustrator:** Lane Smith
**First published:** 1989
**Grade band:** Grade 2 / Ages 5–8
**Format:** Illustrated picture book (a fairy-tale retelling)

---

## Premise

A fresh, funny retelling of the classic *Three Little Pigs* — but told from the wolf's point of view. The wolf, who calls himself **Alexander T. Wolf** (A. Wolf for short), insists everyone has the story wrong. He says he was just trying to **borrow a cup of sugar** from his neighbours so he could bake a birthday cake for his **granny**. He had a bad cold, so when he sneezed, things just *happened* to fall down. The dead pigs? Well, he says, you can't waste a good ham dinner. The book is the wolf's side of the story, all the way to his arrest and imprisonment.

## Setting

The same fairy-tale world as the classic *Three Little Pigs*, but with newspapers, police, and a prison cell. The story moves between the wolf's own kitchen (where he runs out of sugar), the three pigs' houses (straw, sticks, bricks), and finally a prison cell, where the wolf is telling his story to the reader.

## Characters

- **Alexander T. Wolf** ("A. Wolf") — the narrator. A polite wolf with glasses, a cold, and a bad reputation. He insists he has been completely misunderstood.
- **The first little pig** — lives in a **house of straw**. Dies when the wolf sneezes the house down.
- **The second little pig** — lives in a **house of sticks**. Dies when the wolf sneezes the house down.
- **The third little pig** — lives in a **house of bricks**. Refuses to give the wolf sugar and **insults his granny**.
- **A. Wolf's granny** — never seen. She is the reason the wolf is baking a cake in the first place; the insult about her is what makes him lose his temper.
- **The police** — arrive at the third pig's house and arrest the wolf.
- **The reporters** — never seen. The wolf blames them for blowing the story into the "Big Bad Wolf" story everyone knows.

## Plot in order

1. **The wolf wants to set the record straight.** A. Wolf addresses the reader directly: he says the **real** story has never been told. He is telling it now from where he is — behind bars.
2. **The birthday cake.** It was his **granny's birthday**. The wolf was at home baking her a cake.
3. **Out of sugar.** Halfway through, he discovered he was **out of sugar**.
4. **A bad cold.** The wolf also has a **bad cold** that day. He sneezes a lot.
5. **First neighbour.** He walks down the road to ask his first neighbour, a pig, for a cup of sugar. The pig lives in a **house of straw** ("How dumb!" the wolf thinks).
6. **Knock and sneeze.** The wolf knocks. The pig won't come to the door. The wolf feels a sneeze coming. He **sneezes a huge sneeze**, and the **straw house collapses**.
7. **A ham dinner.** Under the straw, the first pig is dead. The wolf reasons: it would be a shame to leave a perfectly good **ham dinner** lying in the straw, so he **eats him up**.
8. **Second neighbour.** Still needing his cup of sugar, he goes to the next pig's house. This one is built of **sticks**.
9. **Same problem.** The pig won't open the door. The wolf sneezes again. The **stick house collapses**. The second pig is also dead.
10. **Another dinner.** A. Wolf insists, again, that you cannot leave good pork lying about. So he **eats the second pig** too and keeps walking.
11. **Third neighbour.** The third pig lives in a **house of bricks**. He is the brother of the first two.
12. **The brick house holds.** The wolf knocks. The pig refuses to give him sugar. The wolf sneezes — but the **brick house does not fall down**.
13. **The insult.** From inside, the third pig shouts an insult about **the wolf's granny**.
14. **Losing his temper.** That is the last straw. The wolf, who has been polite all day, now loses his temper completely. He starts pounding on the brick house, trying to break in.
15. **The police.** While the wolf is going wild outside the brick house, the **police** arrive (along with a news crew). They arrest him.
16. **The headlines.** The reporters, the wolf complains, exaggerate everything. A neighbour just wanting a cup of sugar for granny's cake gets turned into the **"Big Bad Wolf"** of the famous story.
17. **In jail.** The wolf finishes by saying that this is why he is in prison now. He insists that he was **framed**.

## Key objects and props

- **A cup of sugar** — the whole reason the wolf left his house.
- **Granny's birthday cake** — what he was trying to bake.
- **The wolf's bad cold** — the cause of the sneezes.
- **The three houses** — straw, sticks, and bricks.
- **The newspapers** — said to have made up the "Big Bad Wolf" story.
- **The prison cell** — where the wolf is now, telling his version.

## Themes and ideas

- **Whose side of the story do we trust?** The whole book is a lesson in **point of view**. We only ever heard the pigs' version.
- **The news exaggerates.** The wolf blames reporters for turning him into a villain — a gentle introduction to the idea of media bias.
- **Bad luck and bad coincidence.** The wolf claims the deaths were accidents; we are left to decide if we believe him.
- **Family loyalty.** The wolf only really loses his temper when his granny is insulted.
- **Comedy from familiar stories.** The fun of the book is comparing this telling with the version children already know.

## Tone and style

A dry, funny first-person voice. The wolf is reasonable, polite, almost apologetic — which makes everything he describes much funnier. Lane Smith's illustrations are in warm browns and golds, made to look a bit like an old newspaper page, with the wolf in glasses, sweater, and bow tie. The book reads quickly and rewards a child who already knows the classic *Three Little Pigs*.

## Quick fact bank (useful for question writing)

- The narrator's full name: **Alexander T. Wolf** (he calls himself **A. Wolf**).
- Why the wolf is in trouble, according to himself: he was **misrepresented** by the news.
- Why he goes to the pigs' houses: he needs to borrow **a cup of sugar**.
- What he is baking: a **birthday cake** for his **granny**.
- What's wrong with him that day: he has a **bad cold** and is sneezing.
- What the first pig's house is made of: **straw**.
- What happens to the first pig's house when the wolf sneezes: it **falls down**.
- What happens to the first pig: he is killed by the falling house.
- What the wolf does next with the first pig: **eats him** (because it would waste a good ham dinner).
- What the second pig's house is made of: **sticks**.
- What happens to the second pig's house: it **also falls down** when the wolf sneezes.
- What happens to the second pig: he is killed and the wolf **eats him too**.
- What the third pig's house is made of: **bricks**.
- Why this house does not fall: the **bricks hold up** against the sneeze.
- What the third pig says that makes the wolf lose his temper: he **insults the wolf's granny**.
- What the wolf does after that insult: he **loses his temper** and tries to break down the brick house.
- Who arrives to arrest the wolf: the **police** (with **news reporters**).
- Why the wolf says he is famous as the "Big Bad Wolf": the **news reporters exaggerated** his story.
- Where the wolf is at the end of the book: in **prison**.
- The wolf's claim about himself: he was **framed** / it was all a misunderstanding.
